E-Autos Surge 65% in FY26 as Mahindra, Bajaj, TVS Cement Three-Way Dominance

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 2 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/High-speed electric autos (L5) have emerged as the growth engine of India's three-wheeler EV market, surging 65% to 262,683 units in FY2026 while low-speed e-rickshaws (L3) crawled at 4% growth
  • 02/The segment is consolidating rapidly around a dominant trio: Mahindra Last Mile Mobility (35% share, 91,000+ units), Bajaj Auto (33% share, 87,000+ units), and TVS Motor (double-digit share, 27,000+ units)
  • 03/Together they control nearly 80% of the L5 market, squeezing out mid-tier players like Piaggio—whose share collapsed from 11.5% to 5%

India's electric three-wheeler market crossed 823,837 registrations in FY2026, up 18% year-on-year. But peel back the headline and the real story emerges: high-speed electric autos contributed over 100,000 incremental units—more than 80% of total growth. L5 market share jumped from 23% to 32%, while e-rickshaws slipped below 70%.

This is not organic drift. It is structural acceleration driven by product maturation, financing innovation, and a regulatory pivot that has caught policy lagging behind market reality.

Mahindra, Bajaj, and TVS have effectively captured the L5 segment. Mahindra leveraged its early-mover advantage across both e-rickshaw and electric auto platforms. Bajaj pressed its three-wheeler legacy through the Riki passenger platform, rapidly scaling distribution. TVS expanded retail and service footprints to address maintenance anxiety among first-time EV buyers.

The result: Piaggio—once a credible contender—saw volumes crater to ~13,000 units. Mid-tier players Omega Seiki, Euler Motors, and TI Clean Mobility posted respectable double-digit growth (10-29%) but still lost share. The market is rewarding scale, service networks, and financing access—not niche positioning.

Two fault lines are deepening. First, battery chemistry: LFP dominates the organised L5 segment for durability and lifecycle economics, while L3 remains stuck with uneven lead-acid adoption. Second, geography: Uttar Pradesh still drives 35% of total volumes through e-rickshaw demand in Tier-2/3 cities, but western and southern markets are emerging as L5 cargo hubs with stronger fleet operator presence.

The PM E-DRIVE scheme withdrew subsidies for electric autos in December 2025—deeming the segment self-sustaining—while extending e-rickshaw incentives until March 2028. Yet demand is sprinting in the opposite direction toward high-speed vehicles.

Financing is filling the gap. Loan approvals have accelerated through alternative credit models including "no-income document" schemes and battery-as-a-service offerings that cut upfront costs 30-40%. Vehicles now hit 55 km/h with ~300 km range, enabling double-shift fleet operations.

The low-speed segment remains comically fragmented. Market leader YC Electric holds just 6% share (down from 8%), and the top three combined control under 15%. Players like Saera Electric and Dilli Electric—pure-play L3 manufacturers—are now pivoting to electric autos, but from a standing start.

As one industry executive put it: moving from L3 to L5 is not a product tweak. It is a transition from an unorganised, low-entry-bar market to one where automotive-grade quality, distribution density, and service infrastructure determine survival.

FY26 marks an inflection point. The Indian electric three-wheeler market is consolidating around three established OEMs with the capital and distribution to capture high-speed growth. Subsidy withdrawal has not slowed L5 adoption—if anything, it validates segment maturity. The risk sits with L3-focused manufacturers now racing upstream without the balance sheets or brand equity to compete.

Q: WHAT IS THE DIFFERENCE BETWEEN L3 AND L5 ELECTRIC THREE-WHEELERS?
L3 REFERS TO LOW-SPEED E-RICKSHAWS WITH LIMITED SPEED AND RANGE, TYPICALLY USING LEAD-ACID BATTERIES. L5 ARE HIGH-SPEED ELECTRIC AUTOS WITH AUTOMOTIVE-GRADE PLATFORMS, LFP BATTERIES, SPEEDS UP TO 55 KM/H, AND RANGES NEAR 300 KM—DESIGNED FOR COMMERCIAL CARGO AND PASSENGER FLEET OPERATIONS.
