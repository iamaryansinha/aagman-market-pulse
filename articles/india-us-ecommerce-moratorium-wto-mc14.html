India, US Face Off Over E-Commerce Moratorium at WTO MC14 Meet

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 3 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/India opposes US-led proposal to make WTO e-commerce moratorium permanent at the 14th Ministerial Conference in Yaoundé, Cameroon
  • 02/The moratorium bans customs duties on electronic transmissions since 1998; US warns lapse could cut digital exports by 1% immediately
  • 03/India links its stance to demands on public stockholding and farm subsidies; outcome depends on consensus among 166 WTO members

India and the United States are locked in a tense standoff at the World Trade Organization over the future of digital trade. The dispute centers on the e-commerce moratorium that bans customs duties on electronic transmissions.

The moratorium has been in place since 1998. It prevents countries from imposing tariffs on digital goods, software, data flows, and online platforms.

The US is pushing to make the ban permanent at the MC14 conference in Cameroon. The proposal has 22 co-sponsors including the European Union and China.

India has emerged as the key holdout blocking consensus. New Delhi argues the moratorium costs developing countries billions in foregone revenue while benefits flow to developed nations.

India reportedly loses approximately $1 billion annually in customs duties due to the moratorium. The country's digital economy accounts for about 11 percent of its GDP.

The US warns that allowing the moratorium to lapse would disrupt global commerce. Stephen Ezell of the Information Technology and Innovation Foundation said US digital exports could decline "as much as 1 per cent almost immediately."

Kelly Ann Shaw, a trade policy expert at Akin, told US lawmakers that "India in particular has held the e-commerce moratorium hostage for nearly 30 years." India has linked its position to broader demands including public stockholding programs and agricultural subsidies.

Digitally delivered services exports have reached approximately $5 trillion globally. This is nearly double the level from 2017.

The WTO operates on a consensus basis with 166 member countries. Any single member can block decisions, giving India significant leverage.

The outcome at MC14 will test whether the WTO can adapt to the demands of the modern digital economy. Failure to extend the moratorium could introduce new costs and uncertainty for businesses worldwide.

FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS //
WHAT IS THE WTO E-COMMERCE MORATORIUM?
THE MORATORIUM IS A WTO AGREEMENT IN PLACE SINCE 1998 THAT PROHIBITS MEMBER COUNTRIES FROM IMPOSING CUSTOMS DUTIES ON ELECTRONIC TRANSMISSIONS, INCLUDING DIGITAL PRODUCTS, SOFTWARE, DATA FLOWS, AND ONLINE SERVICES.
WHAT IS INDIA'S POSITION ON THE MORATORIUM?
INDIA OPPOSES MAKING THE MORATORIUM PERMANENT. IT ARGUES THAT DEVELOPING COUNTRIES BEAR THE COSTS THROUGH LOST TARIFF REVENUE WHILE DEVELOPED NATIONS CAPTURE THE BENEFITS. INDIA ESTIMATES IT LOSES APPROXIMATELY $1 BILLION ANNUALLY IN FOREGONE DUTIES.
WHAT IS THE US POSITION?
THE UNITED STATES IS PUSHING TO MAKE THE MORATORIUM PERMANENT WITH 22 CO-SPONSORS. IT ARGUES THE BAN IS VITAL FOR AMERICAN BUSINESSES AND WARNS THAT A LAPSE COULD REDUCE US DIGITAL EXPORTS BY AS MUCH AS 1 PERCENT IMMEDIATELY.
WHAT IS MC14 AND WHEN IS IT HAPPENING?
MC14 IS THE 14TH WTO MINISTERIAL CONFERENCE TAKING PLACE FROM MARCH 26-29, 2026 IN YAOUNDÉ, CAMEROON. THIS IS WHERE MEMBERS WILL DECIDE WHETHER TO EXTEND OR MAKE PERMANENT THE E-COMMERCE MORATORIUM.
WHAT OTHER ISSUES HAS INDIA LINKED TO THIS NEGOTIATION?
INDIA HAS LINKED ITS POSITION ON THE E-COMMERCE MORATORIUM TO OTHER TRADE PRIORITIES INCLUDING PUBLIC STOCKHOLDING PROGRAMS FOR FOOD SECURITY AND AGRICULTURAL SUBSIDIES. THIS TACTIC GIVES INDIA LEVERAGE IN THE CONSENSUS-BASED WTO SYSTEM WHERE ANY SINGLE MEMBER CAN BLOCK DECISIONS.
