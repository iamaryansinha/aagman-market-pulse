India Seeks 2.5 Million Tonnes Urea Imports Amid West Asia Supply Disruption Concerns

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 3 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/Indian Potash Limited has issued a tender for 2.5 million tonnes of urea imports with bids due by April 15, 2026
  • 02/The procurement addresses West Asia supply disruption risks as Strait of Hormuz closures limit traditional supplier access
  • 03/Global urea prices have surged 41% from $484 to $684 per tonne within weeks due to shipping cost inflation

Indian Potash Limited (IPL) has announced a major urea purchasing tender seeking 2.5 million tonnes. Bids are due by April 15, with shipments scheduled through June 14, 2026.

The tender targets 1.5 million tonnes for India's west coast and 1 million tonnes for the east coast. This volume exceeds prior tenders, with an unusually extended two-month shipment window.

The procurement comes as West Asia conflicts disrupt traditional supply chains. The effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz has blocked access to key Middle East suppliers that previously dominated India's import portfolio.

IPL will now target alternative sources including Southeast Asia, Oman, the Former Soviet Union, North Africa, and Nigeria. Chinese exports remain unavailable unless Beijing reverses its current export restrictions.

India consumes approximately 38.8 million tonnes of urea annually. Domestic production stands at 30-31 million tonnes, leaving a supply gap of 8-10 million tonnes met through imports.

Urea manufacturing relies heavily on natural gas, which accounts for 90% of production costs. India meets 45-50% of its natural gas demand through LNG imports, much sourced from Gulf producers.

Global urea prices have surged sharply from $484 per tonne to $684 per tonne within weeks. The spike reflects higher insurance premiums and shipping costs through disrupted routes.

The government has prioritised fertiliser sector gas allocation, guaranteeing at least 70% of average consumption to manufacturers. Current urea stocks stand at approximately 6 million tonnes, up from 5 million tonnes last year.

CRISIL Ratings estimates supply chain disruptions could reduce domestic fertiliser production by 10-15%. The subsidy burden may increase by ₹200-250 billion due to rising input costs.
