OECD Cuts India Growth Forecast To 6.1% For FY27 On Energy Risks

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 1 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/OECD cuts India's FY27 growth forecast by 10 bps to 6.1% from 6.2% projected earlier amid global uncertainty
  • 02/FY26 growth pegged at 7.6% while FY28 forecast retained at 6.4%; S&P Global estimates higher at 7.1%
  • 03/Retail inflation seen spiking to 5.1% in FY27 from 2% in FY26; RBI may raise policy rates in Q2 2026

The Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) has cut India's GDP growth forecast for FY2026-27. The estimate was lowered by 10 basis points to 6.1% from 6.2% projected earlier.

The revision comes in the 'Economic Outlook, Interim Report March 2026' titled 'Testing Resilience'. The multilateral body cited the West Asia conflict and surging energy prices as key headwinds.

Growth is expected to ease from 7.6% in FY2025-26 to 6.1% in FY2026-27. The FY2027-28 forecast remains unchanged at 6.4%.

OECD noted that tariff declines should support growth. However, gas rationing will disrupt some production activities. Fiscal support is also expected to fade gradually.

The 6.1% forecast is significantly below S&P Global's estimate of 7.1% for FY27. Industry chamber ASSOCHAM maintains an even more optimistic view, expecting growth above 7%.

"Decline in tariffs should support growth in India, though gas rationing will disrupt some production activities and fiscal support is expected to fade," the OECD report stated.

The evolving conflict in West Asia has led to a halt in shipments through the Strait of Hormuz. Energy infrastructure damage has caused a surge in global oil prices.

"A prolonged period of higher energy prices will add markedly to business costs and raise consumer price inflation," OECD warned in the report.

Inflation is projected to spike sharply from 2% in FY26 to 5.1% in FY27. The FY28 inflation estimate stands at 4.1%.

OECD indicated that India is projected to raise policy rates temporarily in Q2 2026. This move aims to offset stronger inflationary pressures from the energy price shock.
