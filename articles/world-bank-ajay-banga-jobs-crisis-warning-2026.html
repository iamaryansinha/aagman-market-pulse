World Bank Chief Warns Of 800 Million Job Deficit As 1.2 Billion Enter Workforce

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 1 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/World Bank President Ajay Banga warns of 800 million job deficit as 1.2 billion people enter workforce in developing countries over next 10-15 years
  • 02/Current economic trajectory would generate only 400 million jobs, leaving massive shortfall and potential instability risks
  • 03/Spring meetings in Washington to focus on policy reforms, private sector investment, and five key sectors for job creation

World Bank President Ajay Banga has issued a stark warning about a looming global jobs crisis. Over 1.2 billion people will reach working age in developing countries in the next decade.

Current economic trajectories would generate only 400 million jobs. This leaves a deficit of 800 million jobs, Banga told Reuters in an interview.

The warning comes as finance officials gather in Washington this week. Spring meetings of the World Bank and IMF will address the crisis under the shadow of Middle East conflicts.

Banga acknowledged the challenge of focusing on long-term issues amid short-term shocks. The global economy has faced repeated disruptions since the COVID-19 pandemic.

"We have to walk and chew gum at the same time," Banga said. He emphasized balancing immediate crises with longer-term structural challenges.

The World Bank's Development Committee plans to streamline policy and regulatory conditions. Reforms will target permits, anti-corruption measures, labor laws, and land laws.

Five sectors have been identified for investment: infrastructure, agriculture for small farmers, primary health care, tourism, and value-added manufacturing. These areas are less vulnerable to AI disruption.

Banga highlighted successful developing country companies expanding globally. India's Reliance Industries and Mahindra Group, plus Nigeria's Dangote, serve as models.

Failure to address the jobs gap carries severe implications. Banga warned of increased illegal migration and global instability risks.

UN data shows over 117 million people were displaced worldwide as of 2025. The World Bank also announced a push for clean water access for one billion more people.
