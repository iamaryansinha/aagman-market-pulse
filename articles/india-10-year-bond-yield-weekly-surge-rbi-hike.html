India 10-Year Bond Yield Logs Biggest Weekly Surge Since RBI's May 2022 Surprise Hike

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 1 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/India's 10-year government bond yield jumped to 6.96%, the highest level since August 2024 and biggest weekly surge since May 2022
  • 02/The surge is driven by Middle East oil shock, rupee depreciation to record lows, and inflation concerns
  • 03/RBI has cut repo rate by 125 bps since February 2025, but bond yields have risen 30 bps since June 2025 rate cut

India's 10-year government bond yield has surged to 6.96%, logging its biggest weekly increase since the Reserve Bank of India's surprise rate hike in May 2022. The benchmark yield reached its highest level since August 2024.

The sharp rise in yields comes amid escalating geopolitical tensions in West Asia. Brent crude oil prices have surged nearly 50% since late February, reaching $112.66 per barrel and fueling inflation concerns.

The Indian rupee has come under severe pressure, hitting a record low of 94.2950 against the US dollar. The currency has depreciated over 10% since March 31, 2025, on track for its worst fiscal year drop since the 2013-14 taper tantrum.

Despite the RBI implementing cumulative rate cuts of 125 basis points since February 2025, bringing the repo rate down to 5.25%, long-term bond yields have continued to rise. The 10-year yield has climbed approximately 30 basis points since the central bank's 50 bps cut in June 2025.

This divergence highlights how bond markets are repricing risk amid external shocks. Higher oil prices threaten to widen India's current account deficit and reignite inflationary pressures, complicating the RBI's monetary policy stance.

New RBI Governor Sanjay Malhotra has adopted a more flexible approach to currency management, allowing the rupee to move in tandem with other emerging market currencies. This marks a departure from the previous policy of maintaining a stable rupee against the dollar.

Markets are now watching the 7% level on the 10-year yield closely. Some strategists expect the RBI to tolerate gradual rupee depreciation while preventing yields from breaching this psychological threshold.

The rising yields have implications for corporate borrowers and government borrowing costs. The yield curve has steepened significantly, with short-term yields falling and long-term yields jumping, reflecting investor preference for shorter duration debt.
