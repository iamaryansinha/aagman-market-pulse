Gold Price Crash Explained: Why Yellow Metal Fell 21% From Record Highs

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 2 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/Gold has plunged 21% from its January 2026 record high of $5,608/oz to $4,395/oz on March 26
  • 02/US Dollar Index surged from 97 to over 100 since February, making gold expensive for foreign buyers
  • 03/Easing West Asia tensions and profit booking after 70% 2025 rally are pressuring prices further

Gold prices have crashed 21% from their all-time high. Spot gold fell to $4,395.70 per ounce on March 26, 2026, down 2.94% in a single day.

The yellow metal has now lost 16.7% over the past month alone. This marks one of the sharpest corrections in over four decades.

The selloff began after gold hit a record $5,608.35 per ounce in January 2026. From that peak, prices have shed over $1,200 per ounce.

A surging US dollar is the primary driver. The Dollar Index (DXY) climbed sharply from around 97 in mid-February to over 100.15 by mid-March.

Since gold is priced in dollars, a stronger currency makes it expensive for holders of other currencies. This reduces both investment and physical demand.

Peace efforts in West Asia are also denting safe-haven appeal. The Israel-Iran conflict, which began February 27, is now in its fourth week.

Any ceasefire deal would remove a key support for gold prices. Analysts note that geopolitical risk premium is quickly exiting the market.

Profit booking after a stellar 2025 is adding pressure. Gold gained an extraordinary 64-70% last year, its best performance in decades.

After such a sharp rally, valuations had turned stretched. Traders are locking in gains rather than adding fresh positions.

Liquidity-driven selling is compounding the fall. Investors are liquidating gold to meet margin calls and rebalance portfolios amid market volatility.

"Bullion prices are under pressure due to panic selling and fresh short positions," said Navneet Damani, head of research at Motilal Oswal Financial Services.

Silver has fared even worse, plunging 43% from its lifetime high of $121.7 to around $69.30 per ounce.

The Federal Reserve has left rates unchanged at 3.5-3.75%. A hawkish tone from the Fed is supporting the dollar and weighing on non-yielding assets like gold.

Analysts see support for gold at $4,100-4,200. Motilal Oswal recommends accumulation in this range with potential upside to $5,500 once tensions ease.
