West Asia Conflict To Strain India's FY27 Fiscal Math: ICRA Warns Of Higher CAD, Inflation

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 1 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/ICRA projects India's current account deficit could widen to 1.9-2.2% of GDP if crude stays at $100-105/barrel versus baseline 1% estimate
  • 02/Every $10 per barrel increase in oil prices adds $12-13 billion to annual import bill and expands CAD by 30-40 basis points
  • 03/Fiscal deficit faces 30 bps upside risk; government may need to cut excise duties or increase subsidies to shield consumers

Escalating West Asia conflict poses significant risks to India's FY27 fiscal calculations, ICRA has warned. The rating agency expects higher oil prices to strain the current account deficit and government finances.

The Strait of Hormuz handles 25% of global seaborne oil shipments and 20% of LNG exports. India depends on this route for over half its crude supplies.

ICRA's baseline projection assumes crude at $70-75 per barrel for FY27. Under this scenario, the net oil import bill rises to $132 billion from $123 billion in FY26.

However, sustained geopolitical tensions could push prices significantly higher. ICRA estimates that every $10 increase beyond baseline widens the current account deficit by 30-40 basis points.

If crude averages $100-105 per barrel, India's CAD could expand to 1.9-2.2% of GDP. This compares with the baseline estimate of around 1% for FY27.

India's Q3FY26 CAD already widened to $13.2 billion or 1.3% of GDP. The rising trend threatens external stability.

Higher oil prices also risk upsetting fiscal math. UBS India estimates a 30 basis point upside risk to the fiscal deficit if elevated prices persist.

The government faces a difficult choice. It can cut excise duties or increase fuel subsidies to shield consumers. A ₹2 per litre excise reduction costs approximately ₹32,000 crore annually.

ICRA Chief Economist Aditi Nayar said West Asia accounts for 14% of India's exports and 20.9% of imports. The conflict raises freight costs and creates supply uncertainty.

Beyond trade, 40% of India's inward remittances originate from West Asian countries. Geopolitical uncertainty could reduce these inflows, adding pressure to the current account.
