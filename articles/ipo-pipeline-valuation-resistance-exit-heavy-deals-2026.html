IPO Pipeline Faces Valuation Crunch As Exit-Heavy Deals Meet Investor Resistance

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 3 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/IPO-bound companies with high Offer-for-Sale components face valuation resistance as seller-investor expectations diverge
  • 02/Continuum Green Energy's ₹3,650-crore IPO includes ₹2,400-crore OFS; Veritas Finance's ₹2,800-crore issue has ₹2,200-crore OFS
  • 03/Companies nearing regulatory deadlines must choose between cutting valuations or waiting for better market conditions

A clutch of IPO-bound companies is running into valuation resistance as regulatory deadlines approach. The friction stems from high Offer-for-Sale (OFS) components and a widening gap between seller expectations and investor appetite.

Continuum Green Energy Ltd's proposed ₹3,650-crore IPO includes an OFS of ₹2,400 crore. This represents nearly two-thirds of the total issue size.

Veritas Finance Ltd's ₹2,800-crore offering carries an even higher OFS burden. About ₹2,200 crore, or close to 80 per cent, constitutes existing shareholder exits.

Hero FinCorp Ltd's planned issue includes an OFS of roughly ₹1,568 crore. This accounts for over 40 per cent of the total offering.

The shift is visible in the unlisted market, which acts as a forward indicator for IPO pricing. Hero FinCorp shares have corrected sharply to around ₹1,040-1,050 from levels of over ₹2,000 in the past year.

"There is a bit of a mismatch. Investors want to come in lower, but existing shareholders are not willing to take that cut," said an investment banker. "That's where deals are getting stuck."

Investor behaviour has become more cautious across both primary and unlisted markets. "Investors are approaching the primary market with considerably more discernment in FY26 than they were 12 to 18 months ago," said Vijay Kuppa, CEO of InCred Money.

"What we're witnessing today is a recalibration of expectations, with investors demanding a more defensible entry point and focusing more on fundamentals," Kuppa added.

Bankers note the challenge is less about lack of demand and more about pricing. "The capital is available, but not at any price," said another banker.

Most promoters would rather wait than reset valuations immediately. "If these deals were to launch today, many would have to revisit pricing," the banker added.

Kamraj Singh Negi, MD and CEO–Investment Banking at Pantomath Capital, said the market is moving towards a "more calibrated approach". Valuations are increasingly linked to fundamentals such as earnings visibility and cash flows.

For now, IPO-bound firms face a narrowing set of options. They must either cut valuations or hold back and wait for a more favourable market window.
