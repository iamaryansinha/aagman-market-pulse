Sula Vineyards to Develop Luxury Resort at Former Chandon Site as Wine Tourism Hits ₹100 Crore Milestone

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 3 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/India's dominant wine producer Sula Vineyards is converting its newly acquired Chandon facility into a luxury hospitality destination, betting on wine tourism as a serious revenue engine
  • 02/The business unit just crossed ₹100 crore—20% of company turnover—with the company claiming 80-85% market share in Indian wine tourism
  • 03/The move comes after LVMH's Moët Hennessy exited India, selling the underperforming asset to Sula

Sula Vineyards is moving fast on the Chandon acquisition. What was until recently LVMH's failed bet on Indian sparkling wine is becoming Sula's next hospitality play. The company confirmed plans to build a resort at the Dindori, Nashik site, alongside immediate conversion of the existing tasting facility into retail and experience space.

The numbers justify the ambition. Wine tourism at Sula just did ₹100 crore in revenue—one-fifth of total company turnover. The company estimates its share of India's wine tourism market at 80-85%. This is not a side business anymore; it is a core revenue pillar.

Sula acquired the Chandon winery from Moët Hennessy after the French luxury group called time on its India operations. The official reason cited was underperformance. For Sula, it was an opportunistic pickup of a distressed asset in a premium location.

Monit Ravindra Dhavale, Senior VP of Hospitality, confirmed the resort timeline: "Very soon we are going to announce... it is going to take time to build." Immediate priorities are operational—a bottle shop, gift shop, and tasting room are expected within weeks of statutory handover.

Location fundamentals are strengthening. The Dindori site sits near fresh MIDC investments by Reliance Life Sciences and Godrej. Air connectivity to Nashik is expanding with routes to Delhi, Bangalore, Ahmedabad, Goa, and Indore operational, plus Kolkata starting June.

Sula's Nashik campus draws 3-3.5 lakh visitors annually. Four in ten participate in winery tours and tastings. Weekend entry runs ₹1,000. Peak days see 5,000 visitors. Day visitor spending has climbed approximately 50% in recent years.

The company's hospitality portfolio now spans Sula, Source, York, and soon Chandon. The newest property, The Haven, opened October 2024 and sold out through most of the October-February high season, driven primarily by premium weddings.

Sula enforces strict wine purchase requirements for weddings. Dhavale was direct: "If you are not taking wine, we are not talking... why are you doing it in Sula?" This is vertical integration with teeth—use the venue, buy the product.

The tasting room demographics are shifting. Women now comprise 50% of consumers. Younger entrants are skipping beer and spirits, coming directly to wine. Dhavale acknowledged the Gen Z moderation trend but positioned wine as the beneficiary: "Wine is never looked at as a thing where you get high on it."

Product strategy reflects this segmentation. Canned wines target entry-level consumers, but premium lines like Rasa stay out of cans. The boundary between accessible and premium is deliberately maintained.

Sula frames sustainability as business infrastructure, not marketing. By FY26, solar power with battery storage and biogas from winemaking waste should cover nearly three-quarters of energy needs. The company runs closed-loop water recycling, converts grape pomace to compost, and has planted over one lakh trees across Nashik properties in the past decade—including 25,000 last year for its 25th anniversary. Net zero target: 2050.

Sula holds 60-65% of India's wine market and has been category leader since FY09. The Chandon resort, when completed, tightens its grip on both production capacity and the experience economy around wine.

The play is straightforward: dominate supply, control the narrative through tourism, and extract full value from every consumer touchpoint. LVMH could not make India work. Sula is betting it can—by treating wine tourism not as brand marketing, but as a standalone profit center.
