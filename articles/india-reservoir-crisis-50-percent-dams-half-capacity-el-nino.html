India Reservoir Crisis: 50% of 166 Major Dams Below Half Capacity as El Nino Threat Looms

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 2 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/82 of India's 166 major reservoirs are below 50% capacity with overall storage at 46.54% of 183.565 BCM
  • 02/El Nino has 60% chance of forming June-August, threatening monsoon rains needed for replenishment
  • 03/Iran war has pushed urea prices up 40% and DAP up 25%, raising input costs for farmers

At least 82 of India's 166 major reservoirs are running below half capacity. The overall storage stands at 85.432 BCM, just 46.54% of the total 183.565 BCM capacity.

Storage levels remain 10.5 percentage points higher than last year. But with summer approaching, levels will drop further before the monsoon.

The southern region faces the most stress. All five states—Andhra Pradesh, Karnataka, Kerala, Tamil Nadu, and Telangana—have storage below 50%. Telangana is lowest at 33%.

The eastern region shows extreme stress in key agricultural states. West Bengal has only 25% storage, while Assam is critically low at 17%. These are major rice-growing regions.

Central India's Madhya Pradesh storage has dropped to 42% from 50% last year. Uttar Pradesh and Uttarakhand are at 49% and 40% respectively.

The western region has the healthiest levels at 60% capacity. Gujarat dams are at 61%, Maharashtra at 59%.

Compounding the water stress, weather agencies warn of El Nino forming mid-2026. The US Climate Prediction Centre gives a 62% chance of El Nino emerging June-August.

IMD Director-General Mrutyunjay Mohapatra says it is too early for definitive conclusions. But 13 of India's 18 drought years in the past century were linked to El Nino.

The Iran war has created another crisis for farmers. Global urea prices have jumped over 40% in Southeast Asia. DAP prices have risen over $100 per tonne.

India imports 30% of its fertilizers, with 40% coming from the Middle East. Gas supplies to domestic fertilizer plants have been cut to 70% of requirements, reducing urea output by 7-8%.

The government has budgeted ₹1.71 lakh crore for fertilizer subsidies in FY27. Officials warn the bill may rise by ₹10,000-15,000 crore if Middle East tensions continue.

Despite production cuts, current urea stocks at 5.5 million tonnes are up from 4.9 million tonnes last year. DAP stocks at 2.5 million tonnes are nearly double last year's levels.

Rural markets are already feeling the pain. Urea prices have risen 6%, while DAP prices have surged 25% since the conflict began.

With kharif sowing season starting in June, farmers face a triple threat. Low reservoirs, potential El Nino-driven drought, and surging input costs threaten agricultural output and food security.
