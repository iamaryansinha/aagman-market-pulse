Cars for Corn: How Regaal Resources Is Rewriting India's Agri-Procurement Playbook

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 3 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/Regaal Resources offers escalating incentives including Tata cars to farmers delivering bulk maize
  • 02/Company procured 1.7 lakh tonnes in 2025; targets 2.25 lakh tonnes this year
  • 03/Processed corn yields oil, starch, gluten, germ and fiber for domestic and export markets

Kolkata-based agro-processing firm Regaal Resources Limited is turning heads in India's agricultural procurement landscape with an incentive-driven model that rewards maize farmers with automobiles.

The company operates a wet processing mill in Galgalia, Kishanganj district of Bihar. It has abandoned conventional contract farming in favor of the "Regaal Kisan Maitri Programme."

Anil Kishorepuria, Chairman and Managing Director, explained the tiered incentive structure. Farmers supplying 100 tonnes receive ₹20 per kilogram incentive. Those delivering 200 tonnes earn ₹40 per kilogram. Last year, the company handed out Tata Punch vehicles to farmers delivering more than 6,000 tonnes. This year, farmers must supply at least 9,000 tonnes to qualify for an Ertiga car.

The programme operates through a silver passbook system. After KYC formalities, farmers receive passbooks tracking deliveries. Rewards are distributed at an annual gathering in August.

Regaal Resources launched in 2016 and began commercial operations at its Bihar facility in 2018-19 with daily milling capacity of 850 tonnes. The company now plans to expand to 1,650 tonnes daily.

The firm's procurement numbers show rapid growth. From 3,400 tonnes during COVID-19, volumes surged to 1.7 lakh tonnes in 2025. The target for the current year is 2.25 lakh tonnes.

To manage this scale, Regaal employs 35 local coordinators and partners with farmer producer organisations across West Bengal and Bihar.

The processed corn yields corn oil, starch, gluten, germ and fiber. Regaal supplies starch under its own brand nationwide, including to major buyers like ITC. Export markets include Malaysia, Bangladesh and Vietnam.

Looking ahead, the company plans to add liquid glucose and new value-added starch derivatives. However, it has no immediate plans for additional processing units outside Bihar.

Kishorepuria praised Bihar's industrial policy and highlighted the state's annual maize production of 55-57 lakh tonnes. His vision is to make Bihar self-sufficient in corn processing.

The business faces headwinds. The Iran war has driven packaging costs up 20-30 percent, pushing overall input costs up by roughly 10 percent.
