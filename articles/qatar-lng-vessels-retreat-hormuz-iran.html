Qatar LNG Vessels Retreat From Hormuz Strait Amid Iran Tensions

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 3 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/Two Qatar LNG vessels Al Daayen and Rasheeda turned back after nearing Strait of Hormuz on April 6
  • 02/Would have been first LNG transit since Feb 28; Iran attacks knocked out 17% of Qatar's LNG export capacity
  • 03/Asian LNG spot prices projected above $26/MMBtu through June; India and China are major Qatar buyers

Two liquefied natural gas vessels loaded at Qatar's Ras Laffan terminal turned back after approaching the Strait of Hormuz. Ship-tracking data from Kpler and LSEG showed the vessels reversed course on Monday.

The tankers Al Daayen and Rasheeda loaded their cargoes in late February. Al Daayen was signaling China as its destination. Both vessels are controlled by QatarEnergy, Qatar's state-owned energy giant.

Had the vessels successfully crossed, it would have marked the first LNG transit through the waterway since February 28. That is when US-Israel military strikes against Iran triggered the ongoing regional conflict.

The Strait of Hormuz carries approximately 20% of global LNG shipments. Qatar is the world's second-largest LNG exporter. Most of its shipments head to Asian buyers including China, India, Japan and South Korea.

Iranian attacks on Qatar's Ras Laffan Industrial City have knocked out 17% of the country's LNG export capacity. Repairs are expected to sideline 12.8 million tons per year of production capacity for three to five years.

The Pearl GTL plant, operated by Shell at Ras Laffan, also sustained damage. This is the world's largest gas-to-liquids facility. QatarEnergy previously declared force majeure on LNG deliveries.

Asian LNG spot prices are projected to trade above $26 per million British thermal units from April through June. This represents a massive surge from pre-crisis levels.

India sources approximately 53% of its LNG imports from Qatar and the UAE. Both routes pass through the Strait of Hormuz. European buyers receive 12% to 14% of their LNG from Qatar.

A Japanese LNG tanker named Sohar LNG managed to cross the strait empty on Friday. Its joint owner Mitsui O.S.K. Lines confirmed the passage. The company declined to disclose timing or negotiations involved.

Alternative routing options around the Cape of Good Hope add weeks to journey times. War-risk insurance premiums for Gulf transits have risen from 0.125% to over 0.5% of vessel value.
