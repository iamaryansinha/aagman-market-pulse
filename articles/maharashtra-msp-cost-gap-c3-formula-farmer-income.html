Maharashtra MSP Gap: C3 Cost Formula Shows ₹2,414 Per Quintal Shortfall In Paddy

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 2 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/Maharashtra State Agricultural Price Commission data shows C3-based recommended price for paddy at ₹4,783/quintal versus central MSP of ₹2,369/quintal, a gap of ₹2,414
  • 02/Machinery rentals expected to climb 20% in 2026-27; DAP fertiliser prices at ₹27,000/tonne and MOP at ₹34,000/tonne drive up cultivation costs
  • 03/Farmer suicides in Maharashtra reached 767 in January-March 2025; aligning MSP with real costs could reduce rural distress

Maharashtra's farms span 165 lakh hectares, covering 54% of the state's land area. Only 19% of this farmland has assured irrigation facilities. The remaining 81% depends on monsoon rainfall, leaving farmers vulnerable to erratic weather patterns.

The average landholding in Maharashtra is just 1.34 hectares. Even a brief dry spell can wipe out an entire season's earnings for smallholders. This vulnerability is compounded by rising input costs across all categories of cultivation.

Machinery rentals for ploughing and harvesting are expected to increase by nearly 20% for 2026-27. Fertiliser prices have surged significantly, with DAP costing ₹27,000 per tonne and MOP at ₹34,000 per tonne. These rising expenses create a widening gap between cultivation costs and farm returns.

The central MSP framework uses the A2+FL formula, which covers paid-out expenses and family labour. Maharashtra's State Agricultural Price Commission recommends shifting to the C3 framework for a more accurate cost assessment. C3 includes land rental value, interest on farmer-owned capital, supervision costs, and a fair profit margin.

For 2025-26, the C3-based recommended price for paddy stands at ₹4,783 per quintal. The central MSP for paddy is fixed at ₹2,369 per quintal. This represents a shortfall of ₹2,414 per quintal for Maharashtra farmers.

The disparity extends to other crops. Pearl Millet (Bajra) and soybean also show significant gaps between state-recommended prices and national MSP levels. These differences translate into real income losses for farming households.

Four agricultural universities in Maharashtra track cost data from nearly 2,800 farmers across the state. These institutions include Mahatma Phule Krishi Vidyapeeth (Rahuri), Vasantrao Naik Marathwada Krishi Vidyapeeth (Parbhani), Dr. Panjabrao Deshmukh Krishi Vidyapeeth (Akola), and Dr Balasaheb Sawant Konkan Krishi Vidyapeeth (Dapoli).

Maharashtra recorded 767 farmer suicides between January and March 2025. This follows 2,635 suicides in 2024 and 2,851 in 2023. Marathwada region alone saw 269 farmer suicides in the first quarter of 2025, up from 204 in the same period last year.

The state is promoting Natural Farming as a cost-reduction strategy. Shifting farmland to chemical-free practices reduces dependence on expensive imported fertilisers. This approach is particularly beneficial for rainfed areas where irrigation infrastructure is limited.

Soybean cultivation in Maharashtra is expected to decline by 2 lakh hectares in 2025. Farmers sold soybean at ₹3,900-4,400 per quintal last season against the MSP of ₹4,892 per quintal. This market distortion costs farmers an estimated ₹8,500 crore in losses.

Aligning MSP with the real cost of farming requires updating cost calculations and considering regional variations. Supporting low-cost, sustainable practices like natural farming can improve farmer resilience. The goal is straightforward: crop prices should reflect actual cultivation costs.
