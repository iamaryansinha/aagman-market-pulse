RBI Forex Curbs Give Rupee Relief But Cost Banks $3.2M Per Paisa

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 3 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/RBI imposed $100M absolute limit on banks' net open rupee positions, replacing previous 25% of capital cap
  • 02/Rupee recovered from record low of 94.81 to 93.60 on Monday but gains seen as temporary amid Iran war risks
  • 03/Banks face meaningful losses on $25-35B arbitrage positions as NDF spreads surge to 100 paise from 3-5 paise

The Reserve Bank of India capped banks' net open rupee positions at $100 million on Friday. The new absolute limit replaces a previous cap of 25 per cent of total capital.

The surprise move gave the battered rupee brief relief. The currency opened 1 per cent higher at 93.60 on Monday after hitting a record low of 94.81 against the dollar on Friday.

However, the gains are unlikely to last. "Near-term stability is likely but (the rule change) doesn't rewrite the fundamental pressures," said Dhiraj Nim, economist at ANZ Bank.

The curbs target the basis trade arbitrage that banks built up. Collectively, banks held arbitrage positions of $25 billion to $35 billion, according to a source familiar with RBI thinking.

Banks bought dollars onshore and sold USD/INR NDF offshore to capture spreads. The RBI's new limit targets this trade directly.

"By limiting the positions to just onshore positions, the RBI seems to have targeted the popular trade," said Michael Wan, senior currency analyst at MUFG Bank.

The 1-month dollar/rupee non-deliverable forward points surged to 100 paise on Monday. This compares to the 3–5 paise spread at which these trades were initially locked in.

For an estimated $30 billion arbitrage book, each 1 paisa widening translates into a 300 million rupee ($3.20 million) loss. Banks now face "meaningful losses" if spreads stay elevated.

Bankers sought a three-month deferral of the April 10 deadline. They fear "major dislocation" from the sudden rule change.

India is particularly vulnerable to oil price shocks as it imports 90 per cent of its oil needs. The Iran-US war has pushed crude higher, widening India's current account deficit risks.

"If the spread between NDF and onshore markets stays elevated around current levels, banks could be staring at meaningful losses from the trades," said Kunal Kurani, vice-president at Mecklai Financial.

FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS //
WHAT IS THE NEW RBI FOREX POSITION LIMIT FOR BANKS?
THE RBI HAS CAPPED BANKS' NET OPEN RUPEE POSITIONS AT $100 MILLION (APPROXIMATELY ₹938 CRORE). THIS REPLACES THE PREVIOUS CAP OF 25 PER CENT OF TOTAL CAPITAL.
WHY DID THE RBI IMPOSE THESE CURBS?
THE CURBS TARGET THE BASIS TRADE ARBITRAGE WHERE BANKS BUY DOLLARS ONSHORE AND SELL USD/INR NDF OFFSHORE. THIS TRADE WAS PRESSURING THE SPOT RUPEE AS OFFSHORE MARKETS PRICED IN FASTER DECLINES DUE TO IRAN WAR RISKS.
HOW MUCH ARE BANKS LOSING ON THESE TRADES?
FOR EVERY 1 PAISA WIDENING IN NDF SPREADS BEYOND THE LOCKED-IN RATE, BANKS FACE LOSSES OF ₹30 CRORE ($3.2 MILLION). WITH AN ESTIMATED $30 BILLION ARBITRAGE BOOK, TOTAL LOSSES COULD BE SIGNIFICANT.
WHAT IS THE DEADLINE FOR BANKS TO COMPLY?
BANKS MUST MEET THE NEW POSITION LIMITS BY APRIL 10, 2026. BANKERS HAVE SOUGHT A THREE-MONTH EXTENSION TO AVOID "MAJOR DISLOCATION."
WILL THE RUPEE RECOVER SUSTAINABLY?
