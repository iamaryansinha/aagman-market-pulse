Why India's ₹5 Pack Won't Die, It's Getting Lighter

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 3 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/FMCG giants shrink product sizes to maintain ₹5 price point instead of raising prices
  • 02/Soap bars now 35g vs earlier 50g; shampoo sachets reduced proportionally
  • 03/Strategy helps retain price-sensitive rural consumers while managing input cost inflation

FMCG companies are shrinking product sizes to keep the ₹5 price tag intact.

Rather than raising prices, brands are cutting quantities.

A 50g soap bar is now 35g. Shampoo sachets contain 20% less liquid.

The ₹5 pack remains the entry point for rural India.

Over 65% of FMCG sales come from sachets and small packs.

Losing this price point means losing the consumer.

Input costs for palm oil, packaging, and logistics have risen sharply.

Companies face a choice: hike prices or reduce quantity.

Shrinkflation lets them protect margins without sticker shock.

Rural consumers prefer smaller, affordable packs over larger SKUs.

HUL, ITC, and Dabur have all adopted this strategy recently.

The practice is legal if net quantity is clearly labeled.

Consumer activists argue it reduces transparency.

Companies say it maintains accessibility for low-income households.

Expect more lightweight packs as commodity pressures continue.

FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS //
WHAT IS SHRINKFLATION IN FMCG?
SHRINKFLATION IS WHEN COMPANIES REDUCE PRODUCT SIZE OR QUANTITY WHILE KEEPING THE PRICE THE SAME. FOR EXAMPLE, A ₹5 SOAP BAR MAY SHRINK FROM 50G TO 35G.
WHY ARE COMPANIES REDUCING PACK SIZES INSTEAD OF RAISING PRICES?
THE ₹5 PRICE POINT IS CRITICAL FOR RURAL MARKET PENETRATION. RAISING PRICES COULD PUSH CONSUMERS TO SWITCH BRANDS OR STOP BUYING ENTIRELY.
WHICH FMCG PRODUCTS ARE SEEING SIZE REDUCTIONS?
SOAP BARS, SHAMPOO SACHETS, DETERGENT PACKS, AND EDIBLE OIL POUCHES ARE BEING SHRUNK TO MAINTAIN THE ₹5 PRICE POINT.
HOW MUCH HAVE PRODUCT SIZES REDUCED?
SOAP BARS HAVE DROPPED FROM 50G TO 35G. SHAMPOO SACHETS NOW CONTAIN 20% LESS LIQUID THAN BEFORE.
IS SHRINKFLATION LEGAL IN INDIA?
YES, AS LONG AS THE NET QUANTITY IS CLEARLY PRINTED ON THE PACK. COMPANIES MUST DISCLOSE THE ACTUAL WEIGHT OR VOLUME BEING SOLD.
