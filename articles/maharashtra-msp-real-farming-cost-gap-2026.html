Maharashtra MSP vs Real Farming Costs: ₹2,414 Gap For Paddy Reveals Deep Crisis

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 1 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/Maharashtra data shows paddy MSP at ₹2,369/quintal falls ₹2,414 short of C3 cost estimate of ₹4,783/quintal
  • 02/State farms face 20% machinery rental hikes, DAP at ₹27,000/tonne and MOP at ₹34,000/tonne for 2026-27
  • 03/Only 19% of Maharashtra's 165 lakh hectare farmland has assured irrigation, amplifying input costs

Maharashtra State Agricultural Price Commission data reveals a massive gap between official MSP and actual farming costs. The study tracked 2,800 farmers across four agricultural universities.

Paddy MSP for 2025-26 is fixed at ₹2,369 per quintal. The recommended C3-based price stands at ₹4,783 per quintal. This leaves farmers with a ₹2,414 shortfall per quintal.

The gap is even wider for pearl millet (bajra). State prices sit far above the national MSP for the crop.

Current MSP calculations use the A2+FL framework. This covers only paid-out expenses plus family labour.

The C3 framework adds land rental, interest on capital, supervision costs, and profit margins. It reflects true cost of cultivation more accurately.

Input costs have surged sharply for 2026-27. Machinery rentals have jumped approximately 20% year-on-year.

DAP fertiliser now costs ₹27,000 per tonne. MOP fertiliser trades at ₹34,000 per tonne. Both are critical for rainfed agriculture.

Maharashtra has 165 lakh hectares under cultivation. This covers 54% of the state's total land area. Only 19% has assured irrigation coverage.

The average landholding in the state is just 1.34 hectares. Small farmers bear the brunt of rising input costs.

The Maharashtra government is promoting Natural Farming as a cost-reduction strategy. This model reduces dependence on expensive chemical inputs.

The study was authored by an adviser to the Viksit Maharashtra initiative. The author serves at the Chief Minister's Office.

Without MSP reform aligned to C3 costs, small farmers face sustained margin compression. Rainfed regions remain particularly vulnerable.
