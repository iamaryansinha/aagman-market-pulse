India Allows Mining Lease Expansion to Unlock Deep-Seated Critical Minerals

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 1 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/Government notifies amendments allowing mining lease holders to expand into contiguous areas for deep-seated mineral extraction
  • 02/Added area capped at 10% of existing lease or 30% of licence area; auctioned leases pay 10% premium on new output
  • 03/No additional fees for critical and strategic minerals; states must approve within 30 days

The government has notified amendments to mining concession rules. Licensed operators can now expand exploration into contiguous areas.

The move was announced on Monday by the mines ministry. It aims to unlock deep-seated and critical minerals that are otherwise uneconomical to extract separately.

The reforms enable inclusion of contiguous areas in mining leases and composite licences. Associated minerals can now be added to existing leases of major and minor minerals.

The amended rules introduce a time-bound approval process. Mining lease holders can apply for one-time addition of nearby land for deep-seated minerals.

The added area cannot exceed 10 per cent of the existing lease area. For composite licences, the cap is 30 per cent of the licence area.

For auctioned leases, holders must pay 10 per cent of the auction premium on minerals from the added land. Non-auctioned lease holders need to pay an amount equal to the royalty on such output.

No additional amount applies for critical and strategic minerals. This includes deep-seated minerals specified in the Seventh Schedule to the MMDR Act. The incentive targets minerals found in small quantities that are difficult to mine and process.

States must permit inclusion of any other mineral within 30 days of application. The reforms aim to increase domestic mineral supply for industries.

The amendments support Atmanirbhar Bharat goals. They strengthen self-reliance in the mining sector and reduce dependence on mineral imports.

Deep-seated minerals are typically locked in contiguous areas. Extraction under separate leases was often economically unviable. The new rules make such mining financially feasible.
