Trump Shipping Waiver Fails to Boost US Oil Flows; Fuel Exports Hit Record High

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 1 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/Trump's 60-day Jones Act waiver starting March 17 has not increased oil flows between US ports
  • 02/US fuel exports hit record highs in March as refiners ship to Asia and Europe instead
  • 03/Freight rates skyrocketed as Asian refiners bid for vessels to replace lost Middle East supply

US President Donald Trump's 60-day Jones Act waiver has failed to boost domestic oil shipments. Crude and fuel flows between US ports remained flat at 1.37 million barrels per day in March.

The waiver, effective from March 17, was designed to tame fuel prices amid the Iran war. It allowed foreign-flagged vessels to transport goods between US ports.

Instead of increasing domestic shipments, US fuel exports hit record highs last month. Refiners shipped more fuel from the Gulf Coast to Asia and Europe.

Some flows even reversed direction. The US East Coast began exporting fuel to Europe, capitalizing on better arbitrage opportunities.

Intra-US shipments from the Gulf Coast to other coastal markets declined. Volumes fell to 770,000 bpd in March from 826,000 bpd in February.

Asian and European markets face severe supply disruptions. Iran's blockade of the Strait of Hormuz has cut off their regular crude and fuel sources.

This created massive arbitrage opportunities. European gasoil futures traded above $200 per barrel on Monday, while US ultra-low sulfur diesel remained under $185.

Asian refiners are bidding aggressively for vessels. They want to import more US crude to replace lost Middle East supply.

Freight rates have skyrocketed as a result. "We are not seeing any real response or results because all freight skyrocketed," said Tom Kloza, chief energy advisor to Gulf Oil.

The Jones Act normally restricts domestic port movements to US-flagged vessels. Low availability of such ships had been blamed for high fuel prices in California and Hawaii.

The waiver was intended to address this constraint. However, global supply disruptions have created stronger profit incentives for exports.
