Chinese Ships Turn Back From Hormuz Despite Iran's Safe Passage Promise

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 1 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/Two Chinese container ships turned back from Strait of Hormuz after attempting to exit on Friday morning
  • 02/Vessels stuck in Gulf since February 28 despite Iran Foreign Minister's safe passage guarantee for China
  • 03/Iran's blockade has stranded 20,000 seafarers and halted Saudi crude and Qatar LNG exports

Two Chinese container ships reversed course after attempting to exit the Persian Gulf via the Strait of Hormuz. CSCL Indian Ocean and CSCL Arctic Ocean turned back at 0350 GMT Friday despite Iranian assurances of safe passage.

Both vessels are Hong Kong-flagged and operated by COSCO. They have been stranded in the Gulf since February 28 when regional hostilities began.

COSCO had resumed bookings on March 25 for destinations including UAE, Saudi Arabia, Bahrain, Qatar, Kuwait, and Iraq. The turnaround suggests continued security concerns despite diplomatic promises.

Kpler shipping data confirmed the vessels reversed course. Analyst Rebecca Gerdes stated safe passage could not be guaranteed despite Iran's public commitments.

Iran Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi had specifically named China among friendly nations permitted passage. The approved list included Russia, India, Iraq, and Pakistan.

The vessels broadcast AIS messages identifying Chinese ownership and crew nationality. Iranian state media reported Revolutionary Guards warned three container ships of various nationalities to turn back.

Iran's blockade has stranded hundreds of commercial vessels and approximately 20,000 seafarers in the Gulf. Energy exports from Saudi Arabia and Qatar remain effectively halted.

Former President Trump claimed Iran allowed 10 oil tankers to transit as a goodwill gesture. A Thai oil tanker passed through after diplomatic coordination.

Chinese Foreign Minister Wang Yi said peace talks would be conducive to restoring normal navigation. The incident highlights the gap between diplomatic rhetoric and operational reality in the region.
