RBI FX Curbs Widen Rupee Futures-Forwards Arbitrage Gap, Bankers Stay Sidelined

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 1 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/RBI's FX position limits have widened the spread between rupee futures and forwards to 23-24 paisa
  • 02/April dollar-rupee futures trade at 93.4850 while onshore forwards quote near 93.25
  • 03/Bankers nursing arbitrage losses avoid fresh positions; 1-year forward yield hit multi-year high of 3.96%

The Reserve Bank of India's recent foreign exchange curbs have opened a significant arbitrage window between exchange-traded rupee futures and onshore forwards. However, heightened regulatory risks are keeping bankers on the sidelines.

The April maturity dollar-rupee future last quoted at around 93.4850. This is well above the onshore forward rate of approximately 93.25.

The 23-24 paisa spread would typically attract arbitrage-hungry bankers. But market participants are staying away as they nurse losses on existing trades.

"After what the RBI has done, the primary focus is risk management," said a senior treasury official at a foreign bank. The official requested anonymity as they are not authorized to speak publicly.

There is little appetite to do anything beyond managing flows and positions at this stage. The regulatory uncertainty has fundamentally altered trading behavior.

The RBI recently imposed limits on net open FX positions of banks in the deliverable rupee market. This triggered a reversal of chunky arbitrage positions and unleashed heavy dollar selling in the onshore forward market.

The 1-year forward implied yield climbed to a multi-year high of 3.96% on Monday. It has since pulled back to around 3.35%, still sharply higher than the 2.90% level before the RBI's curbs.

Bankers cite other market distortions following the crackdown. These include a spike in the one-day FX swap rate and unusual volatility in the daily fixing, which has moved beyond its typically narrow band.

While the dollar-rupee spot rate has found a base near 93, forward premiums have seen erratic moves driven by unwinding of trades by state-run banks and patchy liquidity.

During the latest bout of rupee stress, the RBI has demonstrated willingness to double down on measures if initial steps fail to deliver desired impact.
