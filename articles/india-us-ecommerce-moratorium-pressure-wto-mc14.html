India Faces US Pressure As Brazil, Turkey Block WTO E-Commerce Moratorium Extension

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 3 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/US Trade Representative Jamieson Greer announced Washington will pursue a plurilateral e-commerce moratorium after Brazil and Turkey blocked extension at WTO MC14
  • 02/The WTO e-commerce moratorium, in place since 1998, lapsed after failing to reach consensus for extension to December 31, 2030
  • 03/India may need to align with Brazil and Turkey to safeguard policy space amid pressure to join US-led plurilateral agreement

The United States is pushing for a plurilateral e-commerce moratorium agreement after failing to secure consensus at the WTO Ministerial Conference in Yaoundé. US Trade Representative Jamieson Greer named Brazil and Turkey as the two countries that blocked the extension.

The WTO e-commerce moratorium was first agreed in 1998 for a two-year term. It prevents member countries from imposing customs duties on electronic transmissions including software, e-books, and streaming services.

The moratorium lapsed at MC14 after Brazil and Turkey opposed extending it until December 31, 2030. The US has now invited trading partners to commit to a plurilateral agreement outside the WTO framework.

India has consistently opposed the moratorium, citing significant revenue losses and lack of clear definitions. Developing countries lose an estimated $10 billion annually in potential customs revenue due to the moratorium.

Europe and North America account for the bulk of digitally delivered services exports, estimated at $4.8 trillion in 2024. Developing countries argue the benefits flow disproportionately to wealthy nations.

A key concern is the absence of a clear WTO definition of "electronic transmission." This ambiguity makes it difficult to assess the moratorium's full scope and economic impact.

"It is particularly frustrating that the WTO could not achieve consensus to make the e-commerce moratorium permanent," Greer said in a statement. The US claims it has secured commitments from dozens of countries not to impose tariffs on digital transmissions.

"If the WTO cannot achieve this commonsense aim, the United States will work outside of the WTO with all interested partners to get it done," Greer added.

India indicated it would accept a two-year extension at MC14, while the US pushed for a permanent solution. A four-year extension was also discussed as a possible compromise.

Experts suggest India should align with Brazil and Turkey to protect developing country interests. "India should support Brazil and Turkey as they stood up for the Global South," said Ranja Sengupta of Third World Network India.

The ending of the moratorium marks a historic moment for digital trade policy. It could signal a shift toward digital industrial policies in developing nations.

Parminder Jeet Singh of Just Net Coalition noted this could mark the start of developing nations properly regulating foreign firms and securing due taxes from digital value flows.

India has argued that customs duties are essential policy tools for developing domestic industries. The moratorium removes this tool precisely when digital trade is expanding rapidly.

The US pressure comes amid broader WTO reform discussions. Trade ministers are working on a roadmap to improve decision-making in the consensus-based system.

Developing countries including India, Indonesia, and South Africa have long questioned the moratorium's fairness. UNCTAD estimated developing countries and LDCs lost $56 billion in tariff revenue from 2017-2020.

A plurilateral agreement would include only a subset of countries. Those outside would not benefit from duty-free treatment but would face pressure to join.

FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS //
WHAT IS THE WTO E-COMMERCE MORATORIUM?
THE E-COMMERCE MORATORIUM IS A WTO AGREEMENT IN PLACE SINCE 1998 THAT PROHIBITS MEMBER COUNTRIES FROM IMPOSING CUSTOMS DUTIES ON ELECTRONIC TRANSMISSIONS, INCLUDING DIGITAL PRODUCTS LIKE SOFTWARE, E-BOOKS, AND STREAMING SERVICES.
WHY DID THE MORATORIUM LAPSE AT MC14?
THE MORATORIUM LAPSED BECAUSE BRAZIL AND TURKEY BLOCKED CONSENSUS ON EXTENDING IT UNTIL DECEMBER 31, 2030. THE US WANTED A PERMANENT OR LONG-TERM EXTENSION, WHILE INDIA INDICATED IT WOULD ACCEPT ONLY A TWO-YEAR EXTENSION.
HOW MUCH REVENUE DO DEVELOPING COUNTRIES LOSE DUE TO THE MORATORIUM?
DEVELOPING COUNTRIES LOSE AN ESTIMATED $10 BILLION ANNUALLY IN POTENTIAL CUSTOMS REVENUE. UNCTAD ESTIMATED THAT FROM 2017-2020, DEVELOPING COUNTRIES AND LDCS LOST A COMBINED $56 BILLION IN TARIFF REVENUE.
WHAT IS A PLURILATERAL AGREEMENT AND WHY IS THE US PUSHING FOR IT?
A PLURILATERAL AGREEMENT INVOLVES A SUBSET OF WTO MEMBERS RATHER THAN THE FULL MEMBERSHIP. THE US IS PUSHING FOR THIS AFTER FAILING TO SECURE CONSENSUS AT THE WTO, INVITING INTERESTED TRADING PARTNERS TO COMMIT TO DUTY-FREE DIGITAL TRANSMISSIONS OUTSIDE THE MULTILATERAL FRAMEWORK.
WHAT ARE INDIA'S OPTIONS NOW?
INDIA CAN EITHER ALIGN WITH BRAZIL AND TURKEY TO RESIST US PRESSURE AND PROTECT POLICY SPACE FOR DIGITAL INDUSTRIALIZATION, OR JOIN THE US-LED PLURILATERAL AGREEMENT. EXPERTS RECOMMEND SUPPORTING THE GLOBAL SOUTH POSITION TO SAFEGUARD REVENUE AND REGULATORY FLEXIBILITY.
