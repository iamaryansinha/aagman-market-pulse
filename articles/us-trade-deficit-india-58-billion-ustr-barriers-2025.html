US Trade Deficit With India Hits $58.2 Billion As USTR Flags Tariff Barriers

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 1 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/US goods trade deficit with India widened 27% to $58.2 billion in 2025 from $45.8 billion in 2024 per USTR report
  • 02/Services trade balance turned into $5.1 billion deficit versus $102 million surplus in 2024
  • 03/USTR flags high tariffs on farm goods, autos, alcohol; NTBs; data localization; regulatory unpredictability

The US goods trade deficit with India jumped 27% to $58.2 billion in 2025. This marks a sharp increase from $45.8 billion recorded in 2024.

Services trade also deteriorated significantly. The balance swung to a $5.1 billion deficit compared to a $102 million surplus the previous year.

The US Trade Representative's 2026 National Trade Estimate (NTE) report identifies multiple trade barriers in India. These include high tariffs, non-tariff barriers, digital restrictions, and unpredictable regulatory changes.

India maintains steep applied tariffs across several categories. Vegetable oils face duties up to 45%. Apples, corn, and motorcycles attract 50% tariffs. Automobiles and flowers face 60% duties.

Natural rubber carries a 70% tariff. Coffee, raisins, and walnuts face 100% duties. Alcoholic beverages attract the highest at 150%.

The report highlights basic customs duties exceeding 20% on drug formulations. This includes life-saving drugs and WHO essential medicines.

Non-tariff barriers cited include import bans, restrictions, and licensing requirements. Quality Control Orders (QCOs) target raw materials and intermediate goods, disrupting supply chains.

The USTR notes the "opaque and unpredictable" nature of India's quantitative restrictions. US exporters face difficulties accessing the market due to sudden policy changes.

Import licences for remanufactured goods require excessive details. Quantity limitations and long delays between application and approval frustrate US stakeholders.

The Bureau of Indian Standards does not fully align with international standards. US stakeholders report lack of consultation during standard development.

Mandatory domestic testing and certification requirements for telecom and ICT equipment create additional hurdles. The US recommends recognizing internationally accredited labs and harmonizing labeling requirements.

Data localization rules for financial services hamper fraud detection capabilities. Local storage requirements prevent service suppliers from ensuring global network security.
