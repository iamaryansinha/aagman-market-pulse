India Wheat Harvest 2026: Output Seen At 113.5-114 MT As Rains, Hail Hit Crop

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 3 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/India's 2026 wheat production revised down to 113.5-114 million tonnes from initial estimate of 115 million tonnes due to unseasonal rains and hailstorms
  • 02/Output still higher than last year's 109.5-110 million tonnes; government maintains record forecast of 120.21 million tonnes
  • 03/Uttar Pradesh most affected with damage in 23 districts; quality concerns emerge as grain filling stage disrupted

India's wheat harvest for 2026 is expected to reach 113.5-114 million tonnes. The Roller Flour Millers' Federation of India revised its estimate down from an initial forecast of 115 million tonnes.

Unseasonal rains and hailstorms struck key wheat-growing regions between March 11-22. The damage hit crops during the critical grain filling to maturity stage.

The India Meteorological Department confirmed sporadic hailstorms across Punjab, Haryana, Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, Rajasthan and Madhya Pradesh. Some damage was also reported from West Bengal and Gujarat, though officials deemed those negligible.

Production will still exceed last year's output of 109.5-110 million tonnes. Farmers planted wheat across 33.4 million hectares, up from 32.8 million hectares in 2025.

Abundant soil moisture from last year's copious monsoon rains encouraged expanded planting. The increased acreage provided a buffer against weather-related yield losses.

"Production will be higher than last year but below our earlier projections," said Navneet Chitlangia. He serves as president of the Roller Flour Millers' Federation of India.

The government maintains a record output forecast of 120.21 million tonnes. Trade estimates have consistently proven more conservative than official projections.

Private survey agency Agriwatch, commissioned by the flour millers' body, conducted the preliminary damage assessment. Their report classified affected regions into high, moderate and low damage categories.

Overall yield loss could reach 1-1.5 per cent at the pan-India level. Highly affected states may see losses of 2-3 per cent according to survey sources.

Four districts across three states face severe 10-15 per cent production losses. Punjab's Rupnagar district, Uttar Pradesh's Bijnor district, and Bihar's Begusarai and Supaul districts fall in this category.

Twenty-one districts across five states report moderate damage of 5-10 per cent. These include Madhubani, Nalanda and Sitamarhi in Bihar.

Uttar Pradesh emerges as the most extensively affected state overall. Damage ranges from high to moderate and low categories across 23 districts, reflecting widespread impact.

Punjab has high damage concentrated in Rupnagar alone. However, 14 districts in total have reported some level of crop impact.

Haryana recorded widely distributed but mostly low to moderate losses. Lodging and moisture stress were the primary causes in this key breadbasket state.

Madhya Pradesh and Rajasthan face low-intensity impacts in isolated pockets. Most districts in these states report less than 5 per cent damage.

"Hail may have caused some damage here and there, but the rains have largely salvaged the crop from searing heat," said Ramandeep Singh Mann. He farms in Punjab, one of India's primary wheat-producing regions.

Farmers in affected districts identified lodging as a major concern. Hailstorm damage to matured crops has also raised alarms about grain quality degradation.

Quality concerns centre on potential lustre loss and grain shrivelling. These issues emerged after temperatures spiked to 35 degrees Celsius just before the rains arrived.

"Unlike in 2022 when temperature rise was sudden and persisted for a longer period in March, this time the unseasonal rain helped cool down the temperature," said Balbir Tyagi. He farms in Uttar Pradesh's Hapur district.

The late-February heat spike had already raised concerns among traders and farmers. Temperatures soared as high as 35 degrees Celsius in several states before the weather turned.

"The crop is expected to be larger than last year, though quality may be a concern in some northern pockets," said Ramesh Garg. He works as a New Delhi-based grains trader.

India ranks as the world's second-largest wheat producer after China. The country grows a single annual crop, sown in October-November and harvested in March and April.

In recent years, late-February and early-March heat has consistently cut yields. Climate variability has emerged as a major challenge for the sector.

After years of weak output, production rebounded in 2025 on favourable weather. The recovery had quelled speculation about India potentially resuming wheat imports.

Unlike rice, India's wheat inventories remain relatively modest. Last year's bumper harvest helped stabilise domestic prices and reduce import chatter.

The Food Ministry has set a wheat procurement target of 30.31 million tonnes for 2026-27. This compares to 30.03 million tonnes in the previous season.

The government procurement programme supports millions of farmers through minimum support prices. Adequate procurement is essential for maintaining public distribution system stocks.
