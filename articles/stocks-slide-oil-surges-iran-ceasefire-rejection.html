Stocks Slide, Oil Surges as Iran Rejects US Ceasefire Proposal

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 1 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/Global equity markets decline as Iran dismisses US ceasefire proposal; Brent crude climbs 4% to near $99 per barrel
  • 02/Tehran denies direct talks with Washington despite Trump's claims; 15-point peace plan rejected over missile program conditions
  • 03/Investors watching Strait of Hormuz developments; Iran parliament drafting bill to impose transit fees on shipping

Global stock markets extended losses Thursday as hopes for a Middle East ceasefire dimmed. Iran's rejection of a US-backed peace plan sent oil prices surging while equities sold off.

Brent crude futures rose 1.5% to $98.72 per barrel. US West Texas Intermediate gained 2% to trade at $92.16.

The Dow Jones Industrial Average fell 0.28%. The S&P 500 dropped 0.37% and the Nasdaq declined 0.84%.

Asian markets tracked lower Friday. Tokyo's Nikkei 225 shed 0.8% to 53,343. South Korea's Kospi tumbled 3.3% while Hong Kong's Hang Seng lost 1.9%.

Tehran dismissed a 15-point ceasefire proposal delivered through Pakistani mediators. Iranian officials labelled President Trump's claims of productive talks as "psychological operations."

The US plan sought limits on Iran's ballistic missile program and regional alliances. Iran deemed these conditions unacceptable and presented counter-demands including full sanctions relief.

The Strait of Hormuz remains a flashpoint. Iranian lawmakers are drafting legislation to impose transit fees on vessels passing through the critical chokepoint.

Approximately 20% of global oil and LNG supplies pass through the strait. Disruption fears have kept crude prices elevated above $90 per barrel.

Tim Waterer, chief market analyst at KCM Trade, noted the brief price dip was a temporary removal of the war premium. "Traders are aware that while the missiles are on hold, the Strait of Hormuz is still far from a clear waterway."

The US is deploying an additional 10,000 troops to the region. Total American forces in the Middle East now approach 60,000.

Macquarie analysts warned that if the strait remains effectively closed through April, Brent could spike to $150 per barrel. The firm currently sees a floor of $100.
