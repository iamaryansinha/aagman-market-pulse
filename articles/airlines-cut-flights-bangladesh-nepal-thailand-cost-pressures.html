Airlines Cut Flights to Bangladesh, Nepal, Thailand on Cost Pressures

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 3 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/Air India and IndiGo have reduced flight frequencies to Dhaka, while Air India Express cut Kathmandu-Bengaluru and Surat-Bangkok routes
  • 02/Rising aviation turbine fuel prices and Iran-US conflict have pushed up airfares and dampened leisure travel demand to South East Asia
  • 03/Domestic ATF price hikes capped at 25% but international flights face doubled fuel costs with no relief measures

Indian carriers are scaling back international operations amid mounting cost pressures. Airlines have cut frequencies to Bangladesh, Nepal and Thailand as rising fuel prices squeeze margins and dampen travel demand.

Air India and IndiGo have reduced services to Dhaka. Air India Express suspended its sole Kathmandu-Bengaluru flight in April, with tickets available again from May. The carrier has also curtailed Surat-Bangkok flights in the summer schedule.

Aviation turbine fuel prices have surged sharply. State oil marketing companies capped domestic ATF hikes at 25% for scheduled flights in April. However, international and charter flights received no relief, with fuel costs doubling for these operations.

The Iran-US conflict and subsequent cancellation of West Asia flights have disrupted traffic flows. Passengers from Bangladesh connecting to West Asia via Mumbai and Delhi have been particularly affected by the regional instability.

Higher airfares have tapered demand for Thailand and South East Asia leisure travel. An airline executive noted that expected passenger demand from these routes failed to materialise as travellers deferred discretionary trips.

Nearly half of seats were sold before the crisis when fuel prices hovered around $80 per barrel. Airlines now face the challenge of recovering substantially higher operating costs from already booked inventory.

The fuel price relief for domestic operations is temporary. Airline executives confirmed they have been told the hike is merely deferred, not waived, with petroleum companies expected to recover full charges in a staggered manner.

"The ongoing conflict in West Asia will continue to weigh on the outlook of airlines. Airlines within Asia-Pacific could face thinner profit margins on escalating jet fuel prices, given limited fuel hedging across carriers in the region," said Isabel Goh, analyst at S&P Global Ratings.

FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS //
WHICH ROUTES HAVE INDIAN AIRLINES CUT?
AIR INDIA AND INDIGO HAVE REDUCED FLIGHT FREQUENCIES TO DHAKA, BANGLADESH. AIR INDIA EXPRESS HAS CUT ITS SOLE KATHMANDU-BENGALURU SERVICE FOR APRIL AND CURTAILED SURAT-BANGKOK FLIGHTS IN THE SUMMER SCHEDULE.
WHY ARE AIRLINES CUTTING THESE FLIGHTS?
AIRLINES ARE CUTTING CAPACITY DUE TO RISING AVIATION TURBINE FUEL PRICES THAT HAVE PUSHED UP AIRFARES AND SLOWED PASSENGER DEMAND, PARTICULARLY FOR DISCRETIONARY LEISURE TRAVEL TO SOUTH EAST ASIA.
WHAT IS THE FUEL PRICE SITUATION FOR AIRLINES?
DOMESTIC ATF PRICE HIKES ARE CAPPED AT 25% FOR SCHEDULED FLIGHTS IN APRIL. HOWEVER, INTERNATIONAL AND CHARTER FLIGHTS FACE NO RELIEF, WITH FUEL COSTS DOUBLING FOR THESE OPERATIONS COMPARED TO PREVIOUS RATES.
HOW HAS THE WEST ASIA CONFLICT IMPACTED INDIAN CARRIERS?
THE IRAN-US CONFLICT AND SUBSEQUENT CANCELLATION OF FLIGHTS TO THE WEST ASIA REGION HAVE DISRUPTED TRAFFIC FLOWS, PARTICULARLY AFFECTING PASSENGERS FROM BANGLADESH WHO CONNECT TO WEST ASIA VIA MUMBAI AND DELHI.
WHAT IS THE OUTLOOK FOR INDIAN AIRLINES?
ACCORDING TO S&P GLOBAL RATINGS ANALYST ISABEL GOH, AIRLINES FACE THINNER PROFIT MARGINS DUE TO ESCALATING JET FUEL PRICES AND LIMITED FUEL HEDGING ACROSS ASIA-PACIFIC CARRIERS, WITH REROUTING ADDING TO OPERATIONAL EXPENSES.
