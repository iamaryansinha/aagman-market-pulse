Natural Rubber Hits ₹221/kg: Supply Crunch and Geopolitical Tensions Drive Price Surge

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 2 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/Natural rubber prices in India's Kottayam market touched ₹221/kg on Monday, driven by a perfect storm of domestic supply constraints and global volatility
  • 02/Extreme heat in rubber-growing regions, farmers holding back stocks, and a sharp ₹25/kg spike in Bangkok prices are squeezing availability
  • 03/West Asia tensions and surging crude oil prices are disrupting synthetic rubber supply chains, pushing demand toward natural alternatives

Natural rubber prices in the Kottayam market climbed to ₹221 per kg on Monday, marking a significant uptrend driven by converging domestic and international factors. The benchmark Indian market, closely watched by the global tyre and rubber goods industry, is reflecting mounting supply pressures that show little sign of easing.

The current lean season has already slashed production volumes across Kerala's rubber belt. Extreme temperatures in major growing regions have further dented output, though recent summer rains in parts of Central Travancore offered marginal relief. Farmers are increasingly withholding stocks from the market, anticipating even higher prices amid strengthening global cues.

George Valy, President of the Indian Rubber Dealers Federation, notes that international rubber prices have surged approximately ₹25 per kg in Bangkok, creating a bullish backdrop for Indian farmers. "The global market strength is encouraging domestic producers to hold inventory," Valy explained.

West Asia tensions are injecting significant uncertainty into rubber markets. Any escalation risks disrupting critical supply chains for chemicals and synthetic rubber imports that India's tyre industry depends upon. Higher crude oil prices—already buoying energy markets—are simultaneously pushing up synthetic rubber costs, making natural rubber relatively more attractive.

Santhosh Kumar, CEO of Harrisons Malayalam Ltd, highlighted the substitution effect now underway. "The complete cessation of synthetic rubber supply due to trade and logistics disruptions has prompted a certain level of substitution toward natural rubber," he stated. This shift, combined with lean season production drops, is creating acute short-term supply pressure.

Domestic politics is adding another layer of complexity. Congress leader Rahul Gandhi's recent campaign promise in Kottayam—pledging a ₹250/kg support price for natural rubber in the first UDF Cabinet meeting if elected, with a phased target of ₹300/kg—is reportedly influencing farmer selling behaviour. Traders believe this promise is incentivizing producers to delay sales in expectation of higher guaranteed returns.

The tyre industry, India's largest rubber consumer, has pulled back from aggressive domestic sourcing due to West Asia-linked supply chain fears. Manufacturers are concerned that shipping route disruptions could choke off imports of essential raw materials including petrochemical derivatives and synthetic rubber inputs.

Market participants expect prices to remain firm in the short term. Santhosh Kumar anticipates that supply normalization and removal of logistical bottlenecks will take time, suggesting elevated prices may persist until geopolitical tensions ease and the lean season concludes.
