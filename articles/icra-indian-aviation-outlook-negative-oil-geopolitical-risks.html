ICRA Revises Indian Aviation Outlook To Negative On Surging Oil Costs, Geopolitical Risks

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 1 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/ICRA revises Indian aviation sector outlook from Stable to Negative on March 27, 2026 citing rising costs and geopolitical tensions
  • 02/ATF prices surge 5.7% sequentially in March 2026 as Iran war pushes crude above $100 per barrel
  • 03/Industry net losses projected at Rs 170-180 billion in FY2026, up from Rs 55 billion in FY2025

Rating agency ICRA has downgraded the Indian aviation sector outlook from Stable to Negative on March 27, 2026. The revision reflects mounting cost pressures and geopolitical instability affecting airline operations.

Average aviation turbine fuel (ATF) prices for the eleven months of FY2026 stood at Rs 91,173 per kilolitre. This is 4% lower year-on-year but still 41% above pre-COVID levels of Rs 64,715 per kilolitre.

March 2026 saw ATF prices spike 5.7% sequentially due to West Asian conflict escalation. Jet fuel now accounts for 30-40% of airline operating expenses, squeezing margins across the industry.

Crude oil has surged past $100 per barrel amid ongoing US-Iran military tensions. The rupee has depreciated 3.2% against the dollar in the nine months through December 2025, adding to import costs.

The Indian airline industry is projected to post net losses of Rs 170-180 billion in FY2026. This marks a threefold increase from Rs 55 billion losses recorded in FY2025.

Interest coverage ratios have weakened sharply to 0.7-0.9 times in FY2026 from 1.8 times in FY2025. Airlines are struggling to service debt amid rising fuel costs and competitive pricing pressures.

Fleet operational challenges persist with 13-15% of aircraft grounded due to engine issues. As of December 31, 2025, Indian carriers operated 865 aircraft with 117 planes idle.

ICRA expects losses to moderate to Rs 110-120 billion in FY2027 if oil prices stabilize. However, a prolonged conflict could keep ATF elevated, extending the recovery timeline.

Airlines have limited pricing power in India's price-sensitive market. Passengers remain highly elastic to fare increases, preventing carriers from fully passing on fuel cost hikes.

The negative outlook signals increased credit risk for aviation sector borrowers. Lenders may tighten credit terms or demand higher collateral for fresh loans to airlines.
