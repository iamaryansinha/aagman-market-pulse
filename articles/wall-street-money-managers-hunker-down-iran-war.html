Wall Street Money Managers Hunker Down as Iran War Fuels Market Turmoil

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 2 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/Wall Street money managers retreat to defensive positions as Iran war enters third week with no end in sight
  • 02/S&P 500 has fallen over 5% since conflict began; Brent crude surged to $112/barrel, up 69% year-to-date
  • 03/Strait of Hormuz closure cuts off 20% of global oil supply; markets pricing "TACO" scenario of Trump reversing course

Wall Street money managers are hunkering down as the Iran war rages into its third week. The conflict has triggered a global energy shock that is testing fragile markets and forcing investors to seek defensive positions.

The S&P 500 has fallen over 5% since the onset of military operations. The benchmark index remains above its 10% correction threshold, held up by hopes of a swift resolution.

Brent crude futures are trading around $112 per barrel. Prices have surged 69% since the start of 2026 as Iran shut the Strait of Hormuz.

The Strait of Hormuz closure has cut off 20% of global oil and LNG supply. This marks the first time traffic has stopped through the vital chokepoint since the 1970s.

Gold has defied its safe-haven reputation, falling 9.6% last week. It was gold's worst weekly performance in 14 years as investors dumped previously winning assets.

The 10-year Treasury yield has climbed to 4.39%. Rising yields signal investor anxiety about inflation and potential Fed rate hikes rather than a traditional flight to safety.

Markets are pricing in what traders call "TACO" — Trump Always Chickens Out. The term reflects Wall Street's bet that the administration will reverse course and seek a negotiated settlement.

"The market is pricing TACO as near-certain and imminent," wrote Craig Shapiro, senior macro strategist at NinjaTrader. Historical data shows geopolitical conflicts typically have modest, short-lived market impacts.

Energy stocks have surged on higher oil prices. Technology and consumer discretionary sectors have led declines as investors worry about inflation and slowing growth.

The MSCI ACWI Index fell 3.2% in the first quarter of 2026. Emerging markets and Japan dropped hardest in March though they outperformed earlier in the year.

Hedge funds have faced their worst monthly drawdown in over four years. Balyasny Asset Management fell 4.3% in March and 3.8% for the quarter.
