PM E-DRIVE e-Truck Scheme Stalls: Only 100 Deployed vs 5,643 Target

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 3 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/Parliamentary panel flags "nil achievement" in heavy-duty EV targets with fewer than 100 e-trucks deployed against 5,643 target
  • 02/₹500 crore PM E-DRIVE scheme extended to 2028 as scrappage rules and high costs block adoption
  • 03/Heavy electric trucks cost ₹1 crore versus ₹25-30 lakh for diesel equivalents, creating ₹70 lakh price gap

India's PM E-DRIVE scheme to deploy 5,643 electric heavy trucks has stalled. Fewer than 100 vehicles have hit the roads despite a ₹500 crore allocation since 2024.

A Parliamentary Standing Committee tabled a scathing report in early 2026. It flagged "nil achievement" in physical targets for the heavy-duty segment as of January.

The broader EV programme has reached 58.6% of its goals. However, the heavy-duty truck segment remains a glaring exception with virtually zero progress.

The economics are prohibitive for fleet operators. A heavy electric truck costs ₹1 crore compared to ₹25-30 lakh for diesel equivalents.

The scheme offers a subsidy of roughly ₹9.6 lakh per vehicle. But scrappage logistics, documentation, and compliance costs add ₹3-4 lakh.

This leaves a net benefit of just ₹5-6 lakh against a price gap of nearly ₹70 lakh. The math simply does not work for most operators.

The scrappage-linked design creates a structural barrier. Operators must retire older trucks of equal or higher weight before accessing subsidies.

The policy assumes fleets have ageing assets ready for scrapping. In reality, 70% of fleets are owned by small driver-operators without surplus vehicles.

Heavy-duty trucks in the 55-tonne category were introduced only around 2018. Most remain in active service, limiting eligible scrappage candidates.

The Ministry of Heavy Industries relaxed rules in March 2026. Operators can now aggregate smaller trucks to meet the eligibility threshold.

EVs accounted for just 1.83% of the commercial vehicle segment in FY26. Heavy-duty e-trucks form a negligible sliver despite 17.7% growth in the segment.

Tata Motors, Ashok Leyland, and Blue Energy Motors have launched products. Supply exists but demand remains absent due to policy friction and cost barriers.

Analysts at ICICI Securities and CareEdge identify FY27 as a potential inflection point. This depends on revised scrappage rules and charging infrastructure rollout.

FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS //
WHAT IS THE TARGET UNDER THE PM E-DRIVE SCHEME FOR HEAVY ELECTRIC TRUCKS?
THE PM E-DRIVE SCHEME TARGETS DEPLOYMENT OF 5,643 HEAVY ELECTRIC TRUCKS WITH A TOTAL OUTLAY OF ₹500 CRORE ALLOCATED FOR THE SEGMENT.
WHAT PROGRESS HAS BEEN MADE ON THE E-TRUCK TARGET AS OF JANUARY 2026?
A PARLIAMENTARY STANDING COMMITTEE REPORT FLAGGED "NIL ACHIEVEMENT" IN PHYSICAL TARGETS WITH FEWER THAN 100 HEAVY ELECTRIC TRUCKS ACTUALLY DEPLOYED UNDER THE SCHEME.
WHY ARE FLEET OPERATORS NOT ADOPTING ELECTRIC HEAVY TRUCKS?
HEAVY ELECTRIC TRUCKS COST ₹1 CRORE COMPARED TO ₹25-30 LAKH FOR DIESEL TRUCKS, CREATING A ₹70 LAKH PRICE GAP. THE SUBSIDY OF ₹9.6 LAKH IS INSUFFICIENT TO BRIDGE THIS GAP.
WHAT IS THE SCRAPPAGE-LINKED INCENTIVE REQUIREMENT?
OPERATORS MUST RETIRE OLDER TRUCKS OF EQUAL OR HIGHER GROSS VEHICLE WEIGHT BEFORE ACCESSING SUBSIDIES, WHICH IS DIFFICULT AS MOST 55-TONNE TRUCKS WERE INTRODUCED ONLY IN 2018 AND REMAIN IN SERVICE.
WHAT CHANGES DID THE GOVERNMENT MAKE IN MARCH 2026?
THE MINISTRY OF HEAVY INDUSTRIES ALLOWED "AGGREGATION" PERMITTING OPERATORS TO POOL MULTIPLE SMALLER TRUCKS WHOSE COMBINED WEIGHT MEETS THE ELIGIBILITY THRESHOLD TO QUALIFY FOR A SINGLE ELECTRIC TRUCK SUBSIDY.
