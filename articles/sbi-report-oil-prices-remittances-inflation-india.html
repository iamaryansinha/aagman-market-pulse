Oil Price Surge May Hit Gulf Remittances, Widen Deficit: SBI Research Report

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 3 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/SBI Research warns every $10/bbl rise in crude oil prices could widen India's current account deficit by 36 bps and push inflation up 35-40 bps
  • 02/GCC countries contribute 38% of India's $138 billion remittance inflows; economists see 5% probability of remittance slowdown if oil prices stay elevated
  • 03/India imports 90% of crude needs with 2 million barrels/day passing through Strait of Hormuz, making energy security a key vulnerability

SBI Research has warned that sustained crude oil price increases could significantly impact India's economy through multiple channels. The report highlights risks to remittances, inflation, and the current account deficit.

According to the SBI economists, every $10 per barrel rise in crude oil prices would widen India's current account deficit (CAD) by approximately 36 basis points. This would put additional pressure on the rupee and external finances.

Inflation risks are equally concerning. The research indicates that a $10/bbl oil price increase could push Consumer Price Index (CPI) inflation higher by 35-40 basis points. This poses challenges for the Reserve Bank of India's monetary policy stance.

The report identifies remittances as a vulnerable channel. GCC countries account for around 38% of India's total remittance inflows. India received $138 billion in personal remittances in FY25, up 15% from $119 billion in FY24.

SBI economists estimate there is a 5% probability that higher oil prices could result in a slowdown of inward remittances. Gulf economies remain heavily dependent on oil revenues, making remittance flows sensitive to crude price dynamics.

"For every ~$10 per barrel increase in crude oil prices may widen the CAD by 36 basis points and lead to a 35-40 basis point rise in inflation," said Soumya Kanti Ghosh, Group Chief Economic Advisor at SBI.

Higher oil prices may also slow economic growth by 20-25 basis points in FY27. In a worst-case scenario with oil at $130 per barrel, GDP growth could plummet to 6% from the projected 7%.

Energy security remains a critical concern for India. The country imports nearly 90% of its crude oil requirements. About 2 million barrels per day of India's total 5.5 million barrels/day imports transit through the Strait of Hormuz.

Roughly 40% of India's oil imports pass through this waterway, which the US Energy Information Administration calls the "world's most important oil transit chokepoint." Any disruption could severely impact supply.

Brent crude prices have already surged from $58.92 per barrel in December 2025 to around $85-89 per barrel in early March 2026. This 50% increase in three months signals mounting inflationary pressures ahead.

India has attempted to mitigate risks by diversifying crude sourcing. The country now imports oil from more than 40 nations and has increased purchases from Russia since 2022.

Despite diversification efforts, prolonged geopolitical tensions could still disrupt trade routes and supply chains. The RBI may need to reconsider its monetary policy if inflation pressures persist.
