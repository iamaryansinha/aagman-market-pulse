Crude Oil Shock Deepens Market Jitters as Hormuz Crisis Hits India's Macros

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 3 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/Brent crude prices surged to $119.50 per barrel before settling at $108, the highest level since the 2022 Russia-Ukraine war
  • 02/Strait of Hormuz closure cuts daily ship transits from 129 to just 4, disrupting 50% of India's crude oil imports totaling 2.5-2.7 million barrels/day
  • 03/Indian rupee hits all-time low of 94.83 against USD; trade deficit could widen by $4 billion monthly; FII outflows reach $17 billion year-to-date

Brent crude oil prices have surged to $108 per barrel after touching a high of $119.50 on March 9, 2026. The spike marks a 50% increase from the start of the year.

The price shock stems from the closure of the Strait of Hormuz amid escalating US-Iran-Israel military conflict. The waterway handles 25% of global seaborne oil trade.

Ship transits through the Strait have collapsed from an average of 129 daily to just 4 on March 7, 2026. The disruption threatens 20 million barrels per day of global oil flows.

India imports 85-89% of its crude oil requirements. Approximately 2.5-2.7 million barrels per day, or half of India's crude imports, transit through the Hormuz route from Iraq, Saudi Arabia, UAE, and Kuwait.

The Indian rupee has plummeted to a record low of 94.83 against the US dollar on March 27, 2026. The currency has shed nearly 4% in March alone and over 5% year-to-date.

India's trade deficit widened to $27.1 billion in February 2026, up from $14.42 billion a year earlier. Petroleum trade could add another $4 billion to the deficit in March.

Foreign institutional investors have pulled out nearly $17 billion from Indian markets in 2026. On March 19 alone, FIIs sold ₹7,558 crore.

The Reserve Bank of India has intervened heavily, selling over $15 billion in March to curb rupee volatility. Forex reserves stood at $187.26 billion in early December.

The India VIX volatility index has spiked above 16, a nine-month high. The Nifty 50 has fallen to an 11-month low as banking, auto, and metal sectors face heavy selling.

Every month with crude near $100 per barrel costs the Indian government approximately ₹30,000 crore in subsidies and under-recoveries. Sustained high prices could shave 1 percentage point off GDP growth.

India's strategic petroleum reserves cover approximately 74 days of consumption, below the IEA-recommended 90-day threshold. The Petroleum Ministry is exploring alternative sources from West Africa and the US.

Aditi Nayar, Chief Economist at ICRA Ratings, said, "West Asia accounts for around 14% of India's exports and 20% of imports. The conflict poses meaningful risks through higher freight costs, supply delays, and uncertainty over energy supplies."

Tanvee Gupta Jain, Chief India Economist at UBS India, warned, "Higher energy prices would weigh on consumer sentiment, real incomes and spending, while squeezing industrial margins for some sectors."

Maulik Patel, Head of Research at Equirus Securities, noted, "Crude markets have entered a geopolitical risk-premium phase. If the war lasts longer and limited volume passes through Hormuz, oil prices can reach very high levels."
