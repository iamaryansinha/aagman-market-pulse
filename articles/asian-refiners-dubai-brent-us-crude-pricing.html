Asian Refiners Switch From Dubai To Brent For US Crude Pricing Amid Record Spike

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 2 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/Asian refiners begin pricing US crude against ICE Brent instead of Dubai after Middle East benchmark spiked to record $169.75 per barrel this month
  • 02/Japanese refiner Taiyo Oil purchased 2 million barrels of US light crude at about $19 above ICE Brent for July delivery, marking a shift from traditional Dubai-linked pricing
  • 03/The move could reduce liquidity for Dubai derivatives market as traders shift hedges to ICE Brent; some refiners have requested Saudi Aramco to change its benchmark

Asian refiners are switching to pricing US crude oil purchases against global benchmark ICE Brent instead of Dubai. The shift follows the Middle East marker spiking to record levels this month, according to three refining and trade sources.

Dubai crude surged to an all-time high of $169.75 per barrel last week. This surpassed Brent, making Middle East supply the most expensive oil in the world.

The price spike occurred after S&P Global Platts excluded three of the five crude grades from its Dubai basket. The exclusion was made in anticipation of a prolonged disruption to shipping via the Strait of Hormuz.

Japanese refiner Taiyo Oil bought 2 million barrels of US light crude via tender this week. The cargo was priced at about $19 per barrel above ICE Brent for July delivery.

The refiner typically buys WTI crude pegged against Dubai prices. Taiyo Oil declined to comment on the transaction.

Other Japanese refiners have also purchased US crude priced against ICE Brent instead of Dubai. Details of those deals were not immediately available as they were done through private negotiations.

The shift to Brent pricing could reduce liquidity for the West Asia benchmark in the derivatives market. Traders are expected to shift hedges to ICE Brent, according to one source.

Asian buyers have just begun booking US crude cargoes for delivery in July this week. The change in pricing benchmark reflects immediate market reactions to Dubai's volatility.

Robust demand from French major TotalEnergies has supported Dubai prices at elevated levels. This has further widened the gap between Middle East and Atlantic basin crude values.

Following the market volatility, some Asian refiners requested Saudi Aramco to change its benchmark. They want the world's top exporter to switch from Platts Dubai to ICE Brent.

Saudi Aramco could not be immediately reached for comment outside office hours.

Platts Dubai is the primary pricing reference for physical crude oil delivered from the Middle East Gulf to Asia. In 2024, a record 21.2 billion barrels of Dubai derivatives contracts traded across futures exchanges.

The Brent-Dubai Exchange of Futures for Swaps (EFS) spread is a key indicator of Brent's premium or discount to the Middle Eastern benchmark. A sustained shift to Brent pricing could fundamentally alter Asian crude hedging patterns.
