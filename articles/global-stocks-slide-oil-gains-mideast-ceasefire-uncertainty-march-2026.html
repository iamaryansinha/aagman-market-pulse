Global Stocks Slide, Oil Gains on Mideast Ceasefire Uncertainty: Markets on Edge

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 3 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/S&P 500 futures drop 0.7% and Nasdaq 100 futures fall 0.8% as Middle East ceasefire uncertainty rattles markets on March 26, 2026
  • 02/Brent crude climbs to $103/barrel and WTI rises above $91 as Iran rejects direct US peace talks and Strait of Hormuz remains disrupted
  • 03/Gold trades near $4,435/ounce and 10-year Treasury yields hit 4.38% as investors rotate to safe haven assets amid geopolitical tensions

Global stock markets slid on Thursday as conflicting signals from Middle East ceasefire negotiations sparked renewed volatility. S&P 500 futures fell 0.7%, Dow Jones futures dropped 0.7%, and Nasdaq 100 futures declined 0.8% in early trading.

The sell-off followed Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi's statement that Iran does not intend to negotiate directly with the United States. This contradicted President Trump's earlier claims that "productive conversations" were underway between the two nations.

Oil prices surged as hopes for a swift diplomatic resolution faded. Brent crude jumped 4.3% to approximately $103 per barrel, while West Texas Intermediate (WTI) gained 4.2% to trade above $91. Both benchmarks had fallen sharply on Wednesday after reports of a US-led 15-point ceasefire proposal.

The Strait of Hormuz remains effectively shuttered for nearly a month now. The critical chokepoint handles approximately 20% of global oil and LNG shipments. Insurance premiums for Gulf-bound vessels have tripled since the conflict began in early March.

Gold prices fell 2.6% to $4,435 per ounce after hitting record highs above $5,200 earlier in the month. The precious metal remains up approximately 18% year-to-date as investors seek safety from geopolitical uncertainty.

The US Dollar Index gained 0.3% to 99.85 as traders sought refuge in the greenback. Ten-year Treasury yields climbed to 4.38%, reflecting both safe haven demand and inflation concerns from elevated energy prices.

President Trump issued a stern warning on Truth Social, stating Iranian negotiators "better get serious soon, before it is too late." Meanwhile, Israel claimed to have killed Iran's naval commander Alireza Tangsiri, who orchestrated the Hormuz blockade.

Asian markets face acute energy shortages due to heavy reliance on Middle East imports. Japan announced the release of 80 million barrels from strategic reserves starting March 26, coordinated with Saudi Arabia, UAE, and Kuwait.

Goldman Sachs analysts estimate the conflict has added a $15-20 per barrel risk premium to crude prices. The investment bank raised its 2026 Brent forecast to $85 per barrel, citing the biggest supply shock in history.

Analysts warn that a prolonged Hormuz closure could force a 5-7% reduction in final energy use across hardest-hit Asian nations. The International Energy Agency described the situation as the greatest global energy security challenge in history.
