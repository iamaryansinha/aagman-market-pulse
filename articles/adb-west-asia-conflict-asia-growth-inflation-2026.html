ADB: West Asia Conflict Could Cut Asia Growth by 1.3%, Spike Inflation by 3.2% in 2026-27

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 2 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/ADB projects prolonged West Asia conflict could reduce developing Asia-Pacific growth by 1.3 percentage points over 2026-2027
  • 02/Inflation in the region could spike by 3.2 percentage points if energy market disruptions persist beyond one year
  • 03/Strait of Hormuz carries 20% of global oil and LNG trade; 84% of oil and 83% of LNG bound for Asia

The Asian Development Bank has issued a stark warning on the economic fallout from the ongoing West Asia conflict. A prolonged disruption could reduce growth in developing Asia and the Pacific by 1.3 percentage points and raise inflation by 3.2 percentage points over 2026-2027.

The Manila-based lender released its analysis on March 26, 2026. The report highlights the region's acute vulnerability to energy supply shocks despite limited direct trade exposure to conflict zones.

The conflict, which escalated following joint US-Israel military strikes on Iran on February 28, 2026, has severely disrupted the Strait of Hormuz. This critical chokepoint handles approximately 20% of global oil and LNG trade.

Asia bears the brunt of this disruption. About 84% of crude oil and 83% of LNG passing through the strait is destined for Asian markets. China, India, Japan, and South Korea together account for roughly 70% of all Hormuz crude flows.

Oil markets have reacted violently. Brent crude surged from approximately $72 per barrel before the conflict to nearly $126 at peak tensions. Under ADB's baseline scenario of a 1-2 month conflict, prices average $72 in 2026. However, under a severe scenario lasting one year, oil could spike above $155 per barrel in Q2 2026.

The transmission channels are multi-faceted. Higher energy prices directly impact household incomes and manufacturing costs. Supply chain disruptions delay production of critical inputs including semiconductors, fertilizers, and petrochemicals. Financial conditions have tightened with declining equity markets and rising bond yields.

Regional impacts vary significantly. Developing Southeast Asia and the Pacific face the steepest growth losses at 2.3 and 2.2 percentage points respectively under prolonged conflict. India, sourcing roughly 50% of crude imports via Hormuz, faces acute energy security risks. Japan depends on the Middle East for 90% of oil needs.

The ADB notes that Asia's fragmented energy security coordination limits crisis response. Major consumers like China and India are not part of the International Energy Agency framework. This hampers coordinated emergency oil reserve releases.

Beyond energy, remittance-dependent economies face additional pressure. Weaker economic activity in Gulf economies could reduce labor demand and incomes of migrant workers. This would lower remittance flows to countries like Bangladesh, Nepal, and the Philippines.

"The duration of disruptions will ultimately determine the severity of the economic fallout," said ADB Chief Economist Albert Park. He emphasized that while contained scenarios remain manageable, persistent disruptions could materially weaken growth prospects.

The ADB advises policymakers to safeguard macroeconomic stability, manage energy consumption, and accelerate diversification. It recommends targeted fiscal support over broad-based subsidies to maintain fiscal discipline while protecting vulnerable households.
