Geopolitics Overtakes Economics: Global Trade Faces $115 Oil Shock On Red Sea, Hormuz Crisis

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 3 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/Geopolitics has overtaken economics as the primary driver of global trade flows, with West Asia instability choking critical maritime chokepoints
  • 02/Strait of Hormuz ship traffic plunged 95% to 138 vessels; 170+ container ships stranded in Persian Gulf with $450,000 TEU capacity
  • 03/Brent crude surged to $115-119 per barrel; global oil supply gap estimated at 9-14 million barrels per day

Global trade is no longer governed by supply and demand curves. It now moves to the rhythm of missile strikes and diplomatic ultimatums.

The Strait of Hormuz and Red Sea have become flashpoints that are reshaping how goods flow across the world. Shipping executives now check geopolitical risk dashboards more than freight rate indices.

The numbers tell a stark story. Ship traffic through the Strait of Hormuz has collapsed by 95% since March 2026. Only 138 vessels now transit compared to normal volumes.

More than 170 container ships with 450,000 TEU capacity are stranded in the Persian Gulf. Major carriers including MSC, CMA CGM, and Hapag-Lloyd have suspended all bookings to the region.

The economic impact is immediate and severe. Brent crude prices have surged to $115-119 per barrel, creating a supply gap of 9-14 million barrels per day.

Freight rates have exploded across global routes. MSC charges $2,400 per TEU and $4,000 per FEU for Asia-Northern Europe. Asia-Mediterranean routes hit $4,150 per TEU and $5,500 per FEU.

War risk insurance premiums have jumped 200-400% for Persian Gulf routes. As of March 5, 2026, standard insurance coverage for vessels expired, forcing carriers to pay additional levies or reroute.

Rerouting via the Cape of Good Hope adds $400,000 to $1 million per voyage. Transit times extend by 7-14 days, creating cascading delays across supply chains.

Iran has implemented a selective passage policy allowing only non-hostile nations. India, China, Pakistan, and Thailand have secured passage. Japan is still awaiting clearance.

The crisis highlights a fundamental shift in global commerce. Supply chains must now be built for geopolitical resilience, not just cost efficiency.
