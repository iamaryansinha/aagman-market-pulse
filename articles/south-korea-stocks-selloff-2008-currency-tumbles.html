South Korea Hit by Steepest Stocks Selloff Since 2008

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 1 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/KOSPI plunged 19% in March, worst monthly performance since 2008 financial crisis
  • 02/Foreigners sold record $23.5 billion in KOSPI shares; won collapsed past 1,500 per dollar
  • 03/Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix bore the brunt of selling

South Korean markets buckled on Tuesday, with the benchmark KOSPI sliding toward its worst monthly performance since the global financial crisis and the won sinking to post-crisis lows.

The KOSPI sank 4.3% on Tuesday. Its monthly drop of 19% marks the largest since 2008. The index has now fallen 19.9% from its late February record closing high, flirting with bear market territory.

The won slumped approximately 1% to trade weaker than 1,500 per dollar. These levels were previously breached only in the aftermath of the 2008 global financial crisis and the late 1990s Asian crisis.

Foreigners sold a net 35.9 trillion won ($23.5 billion) in KOSPI shares this month. Exchange data confirms this as the largest outflow on record. The selling has intensified pressure on the currency.

The rout centers on positioning-driven de-risking rather than fundamental re-evaluation of growth prospects. Goldman Sachs analysis reveals foreign selling concentrated heavily in market-darling chipmakers.

Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix absorbed the brunt of outflows, driving foreign ownership in the pair to its lowest level since 2022. Samsung Electronics shed 5.2% Tuesday. SK Hynix plunged 7.6%. Each has dropped more than 20% through March.

Ironically, the magnitude of pre-rally gains has left both stocks and the broader index higher for the year. The KOSPI remains up approximately 20% year-to-date despite the March carnage.

The market's earlier strength has amplified the current pain. Soaring energy prices and fading risk tolerance have left global investors with nowhere to hide.
