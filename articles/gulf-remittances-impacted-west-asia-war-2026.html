Gulf Remittances Face Near-Term Pressure as Indian Workers Hold Money Amid War

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 1 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/Indian workers in Gulf are holding money instead of sending home due to West Asia conflict, impacting near-term remittance flows
  • 02/GCC economies host nearly half of Indian migrants worldwide and account for 38% of India's total remittances worth $36.9 billion in Q3 FY26
  • 03/Government monitoring migrant movement; prolonged war could trigger return of workers and further squeeze remittance inflows

Remittances from Gulf countries are likely to face near-term pressure as Indian workers hold onto money instead of transferring it home. Sources indicate that various refineries and establishments employing large numbers of Indian workers are currently non-functional.

Senior functionaries continue to be stationed in the region. However, lower-level workers have moved from their work locations amid the ongoing West Asia conflict.

This behavioral shift in money transfers could immediately impact remittance flows to India. Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC) economies account for approximately 38 per cent of India's total remittances.

According to a Finance Ministry report, personal transfer receipts reached $36.9 billion in Q3 FY26. This marks an increase from $35.1 billion in the corresponding period of FY25.

The Gulf region hosts nearly half of all Indian migrants worldwide. Any sustained disruption to employment or economic activity in these economies directly affects millions of Indian households.

Rising crude oil prices pose an additional risk to fiscal conditions in GCC nations. This could further moderate remittance growth if economic conditions deteriorate in host countries.

The government is closely monitoring the situation. If the war continues, sources indicate that Indian workers are likely to return home, amplifying the impact on remittances.

Credit availability to industries remains unaffected currently. The government also does not anticipate near-term inflation pressure from the conflict.
