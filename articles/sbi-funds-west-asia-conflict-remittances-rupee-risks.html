SBI Funds Warns West Asia Conflict Threatens Remittances, Rupee, Fiscal Health

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 3 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/SBI Funds report warns West Asia conflict could disrupt India's $138 billion remittance inflows from GCC countries
  • 02/Rupee has already breached 93 per dollar; could slide to 96 if hostilities persist and oil prices spike
  • 03/Every $10 per barrel oil increase could widen current account deficit by 36 bps and push inflation up 35-40 bps

SBI Funds Management has issued a stark warning on escalating West Asia tensions. The report flags severe risks to India's remittances, currency stability, and fiscal health.

India received $138 billion in personal remittances in FY25. This marked a 15% rise from $119 billion in FY24. Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC) countries account for 38% of these inflows.

The Indian rupee has already breached 93 per dollar for the first time. SBI economists warn it could slide further to 96 per dollar in a prolonged conflict scenario.

Around 2 million barrels per day of India's crude imports transit through the Strait of Hormuz. This represents roughly 40% of total oil imports passing through the world's most critical energy chokepoint.

SBI estimates every $10 per barrel increase in oil prices could widen India's current account deficit by 36 basis points. The same price spike could push inflation higher by 35-40 basis points.

GDP growth could slow by 20-25 basis points if oil prices remain elevated. In a worst-case scenario with crude at $130 per barrel, growth could fall to 6% from a baseline 7% projection for FY27.

Foreign institutional investors have pulled out approximately $14.3 billion in FY26 so far. March alone witnessed outflows nearing $11.8 billion, potentially the highest monthly exodus since the 1991 balance-of-payments crisis.

SBI economists estimate a 5% probability that higher oil prices may lead to a slowdown in inward remittances. India's economic health depends heavily on Gulf employment for millions of Indian workers.
