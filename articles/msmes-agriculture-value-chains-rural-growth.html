MSMEs in Agriculture Value Chains: Unlocking Rural India's Growth Potential

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 1 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/MSMEs contribute nearly 30% of India's GDP and approximately 45% of exports while employing millions across sectors
  • 02/Small food processors and cold storage units act as crucial links between smallholder farmers and larger markets
  • 03/Uneven credit access and patchy logistics remain key constraints requiring policy attention to formalisation and enterprise clusters

Micro, Small and Medium Enterprises (MSMEs) contribute nearly 30% of India's GDP and around 45% of exports. They employ millions across manufacturing and services sectors.

Rajeev Singh, Director-General of the Indian Chamber of Commerce, emphasizes their role in rural transformation. Their proximity to local markets enables quick response to demand.

MSMEs collaborate closely with farming communities. Small food processors, packaging units, cold storage operators, and transport businesses shape how produce reaches markets.

A small processing unit buying tomatoes from nearby villages stabilises local demand. Dairy processing enterprises sourcing milk from farmer clusters improve price discovery mechanisms.

Better storage and transport facilities reduce post-harvest losses. This ensures more produce reaches markets in saleable condition, directly benefiting rural incomes.

The World Bank notes that small and medium agribusinesses serve as critical connectors. They link smallholder farmers to larger domestic and export markets.

India's food processing sector has expanded steadily over the past decade. Small and medium enterprises form the backbone of this growth story.

However, significant constraints persist. Uneven credit access, patchy logistics infrastructure, and limited cold storage plague many agricultural belts.

Recent policy focus aims at MSME formalisation, improved credit access, and enterprise clusters. These measures target bridging the rural-urban economic divide.
