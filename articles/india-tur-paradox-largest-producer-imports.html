India Tur Paradox: Largest Producer Yet Spends $3.75B On Imports

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 3 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/India produces 77.6% of world's pigeon pea yet imported 7.71 lakh tonnes in 2023-24, spending USD 3.75 billion
  • 02/Supply-demand gap: Production ~25M tonnes vs demand exceeding by 5-6M tonnes annually; tur trading 14-24% below MSP
  • 03/Policy failures include MSP-import contradiction, procurement gaps, and duty oscillations that destroy price signals

India is the world's largest producer of pigeon pea (tur/arhar dal), accounting for 77.6% of global output. Yet in 2023-24, the country imported 7.71 lakh tonnes, spending USD 3.75 billion on the same pulse it grows most.

This paradox has persisted for decades. India is simultaneously the largest producer, largest consumer, and largest importer of pulses globally.

Production has declined from a peak of 4.34 million tonnes to approximately 3.5 million tonnes currently. Meanwhile, consumption grows steadily with rising incomes and dietary diversification.

The supply-demand gap is stark. India produces approximately 25 million tonnes of all pulses annually. Demand exceeds this by 5-6 million tonnes. FY25 saw record pulse imports of 6.7-7.3 million tonnes, a nine-year high.

The economic cost is significant. Annual foreign exchange outflow on pulse imports alone reaches USD 5.5 billion.

Policy failures compound the problem. The government announces Minimum Support Prices to incentivize farmers. Then it allows duty-free imports that depress market prices below MSP. Tur currently trades 14-24% below its MSP.

Procurement is another mirage. Less than 30% of output is actually procured at MSP. Infrastructure gaps and quality check weaponization keep farmers away from government buyers.

Import duties have oscillated for decades, destroying price signals. The policy bias favors consumers with immediate import liberalization when prices rise. Farmer protection receives sluggish response when prices crash.

Yield potential remains untapped. Current yields average 859 kg per hectare against a potential of 2,500 kg per hectare with proper inputs and practices.

The 2025-26 production outlook is bleak. Excessive monsoon is expected to reduce output by 20%, widening the import dependence further.

WHY DOES INDIA IMPORT TUR IF IT IS THE LARGEST PRODUCER?
DOMESTIC DEMAND EXCEEDS PRODUCTION BY 5-6 MILLION TONNES ANNUALLY. RISING INCOMES AND DIETARY DIVERSIFICATION HAVE INCREASED CONSUMPTION FASTER THAN PRODUCTION GROWTH.
WHAT IS THE MSP-IMPORT CONTRADICTION?
THE GOVERNMENT ANNOUNCES MINIMUM SUPPORT PRICES TO INCENTIVIZE FARMERS, THEN ALLOWS DUTY-FREE IMPORTS THAT DEPRESS MARKET PRICES BELOW MSP. TUR CURRENTLY TRADES 14-24% BELOW MSP.

LESS THAN 30% OF OUTPUT IS ACTUALLY PROCURED AT MSP. INFRASTRUCTURE GAPS AND QUALITY CHECK WEAPONIZATION PREVENT FARMERS FROM ACCESSING GOVERNMENT BUYERS.
