Asian Shares Fall as Iran War Drives Oil Prices Above $105 Per Barrel

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 3 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/Asian shares decline sharply following Wall Street's worst weekly streak in nearly 4 years as Iran war intensifies
  • 02/Brent crude oil prices surge above $105 per barrel, up from $70 pre-war levels, threatening global supply chains
  • 03/Wall Street completed fifth consecutive losing week, raising fears of stagflation and broader economic slowdown

Asian stock markets declined sharply on March 30, 2026, as soaring oil prices driven by the war in Iran rattled investor confidence. The declines follow Wall Street's fifth consecutive losing week, its longest streak in nearly four years.

Brent crude oil climbed 3.4% on Friday to settle at $105.32 per barrel, up from roughly $70 before the war began. Benchmark U.S. crude rose 5.5% to settle at $99.64 per barrel.

Japan's Nikkei 225 index plunged 4.8% at the start of trading earlier this week. South Korea's Kospi index plummeted more than 5.5% as investors fled risk assets.

The conflict has disrupted approximately 20 million barrels of oil per day. The International Energy Agency calls this the largest supply disruption in global oil market history.

Iran effectively closed the Strait of Hormuz on February 28, blocking a transit point for a fifth of world's oil. This blockade has cut traffic from 138 to just 5 ships daily.

Qatar's Ras Laffan natural gas terminal, producing 20% of global LNG, was struck on March 18. The attack wiped out 17% of Qatar's export capacity with repairs expected to take up to five years.

"Historically, oil price shocks like this have led to global recessions," said Christopher Knittel, energy economist at MIT. Infrastructure damage means war ramifications will be long-lived.

The South Korean won hit its lowest level since 2009, falling to 1,510 against the U.S. dollar. This 17-year low reflects panic gripping regional financial markets.

Central banks worldwide are recalibrating monetary policy due to inflation pressures. The Federal Reserve faces conflicting mandates between controlling inflation and supporting growth.

Economists warn the war raises stagflation risks. Higher inflation combined with lower growth threatens the global economic outlook for 2026.
