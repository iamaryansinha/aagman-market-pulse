LPG Shortage Hits Tea Demand at Kochi Auctions as Eateries Shut, Prices Fall

BY AAGMAN DESK | EST. READ: 3 MIN
EXECUTIVE TLDR //
  • 01/Tea demand weakened at Kochi auctions as LPG shortage forced 40% of Kerala restaurants and tea stalls to shut operations
  • 02/Popular liquoring grades fell ₹3-5 per kg, medium varieties declined ₹2-3, CTC dust dropped ₹5/kg with only 64% absorption
  • 03/Export interest limited to lower-end teas; wedding season bookings from March 15 face uncertainty amid supply crisis

The ongoing LPG shortage has dampened tea demand at the Kochi auction market this week. Reduced operations of hotels and roadside eateries led to a sharp decline in local buying.

Purchases weakened significantly with many restaurants and tea stalls remaining shut. The disruption is impacting overall consumption across Kerala.

Anil George, president of the Tea Trade Association of Cochin, confirmed bazaar demand declined amid LPG shortages affecting tea outlets. Export interest was limited to lower-end teas.

Market sentiment has shifted decisively bearish. Tightening demand and external disruptions are exerting clear downward pressure on prices.

At Sale 13, the CTC dust market dropped by around ₹5 per kg. Blenders absorbed only 64 per cent of the 6,57,741 kg on offer, according to Forbes, Ewart & Figgis.

Good liquoring teas saw prices fall by ₹2 to ₹3. Powdery grades declined even more despite an 88 per cent demand.

Popular liquoring grades eased by ₹3-5 per kg. Medium and plainer varieties declined by ₹2-3 per kg.

The orthodox dust segment also declined. Exporters and upcountry buyers took up only small quantities of the 10,500 kg offered at the auctions.

In orthodox leaf, unrest in West Asia led to selective, subdued demand in Gulf markets. CIS and neighbouring countries remained active in purchasing.

The offered quantity was 1,62,652 kg. Whole leaf and brokens were firm to dearer, while the rest were irregular and lower.

The CTC leaf market was good, with medium brokens performing well. Fannings were irregular and lower. The offered quantity was 38,500 kg with a sales percentage of 72.

Around 40 per cent of restaurants in Kerala have been forced to shut down due to the LPG shortage. G Jayapal, president of the Kerala Hotel and Restaurant Association, said establishments are unable to switch to alternative cooking methods.

The crisis comes just as the marriage season is set to begin in Kerala from March 15. The Malayalam month Medam marks the start of peak wedding bookings.

Apart from biryani cooking, most food prepared by caterers depends on LPG. V Sunukumar, district president of the All Kerala Caterers Association, said the sector faces difficulties as bookings have already been made.

Private LPG suppliers are charging almost double the price for cylinders. A 17 kg cylinder was reportedly supplied for ₹3,000 against the normal rate of ₹1,800 for a 19 kg cylinder.

The Kerala government has urged the Centre to increase the quota of non-domestic LPG cylinders allocated to the state. Currently, the quota is limited to 20 per cent.

Hospitals, old-age homes, orphanages, schools, community kitchens and canteens in IT parks will be given priority in LPG supply. The Civil Supplies Department will improve kerosene supply as an alternative fuel.
